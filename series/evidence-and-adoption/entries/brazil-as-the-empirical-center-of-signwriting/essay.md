# Brazil as the Empirical Center of SignWriting

**Documented institutional density, research opportunity, and why Brazil matters**

This entry is the first major anchor of *Sutton SignWriting Evidence and Adoption*.

Its task is straightforward:

- treat Brazil as a first-class empirical center
- separate documented evidence from hopeful projection
- show why Brazil matters for future science, translation, partnership, and infrastructure

Brazil should not be treated as a side example in SignWriting conversations.

It is the strongest documented empirical center in the current public record.

That statement does not depend on claiming that every detail has already been counted or that every part of the Brazilian story has been fully told.

It depends on something simpler:

- Brazil shows repeated institutional use rather than isolated anecdote
- Brazil has both school-based and university-based SignWriting activity
- Brazil has a substantial research record
- Brazil provides a realistic starting point for future literacy, cognition, corpus, and infrastructure work

The public SignWriting record also shows that this did not appear suddenly.

The Brazil archive on SignWriting.org links historical materials, classrooms, dictionaries, literature, courses, and university references, and the symposium archive preserves a long run of Brazil-centered presentations in education, research, software, and literacy.

At the current public count, the symposium archive alone includes **37 Brazil-related presentations** from 2014 to 2024.

For this count, "Brazil-related" means presentations in the public SignWriting Symposium archive whose title, abstract, presenter affiliation, or topic clearly identifies Brazilian SignWriting, Libras, Brazilian institutions, Brazilian education, or Brazilian research contexts.

That count is a working count from the public SignWriting Symposium presentation archive. It should be treated as a transparent archive count, not as a bibliometric study or a deduplicated national publication dataset.

## Why Brazil deserves separate treatment

Much of the international discussion around SignWriting still treats use cases as scattered examples.

Brazil no longer fits that pattern.

The public record already shows enough institutional density to justify treating Brazil as a major empirical center in its own right.

That matters because it changes the starting point for scientific and institutional conversations.

Instead of asking whether SignWriting could ever support real educational and research use, a more serious question becomes possible:

> How should the strongest existing SignWriting ecosystem be documented, studied, and supported?

## The 2018 documented floor

**Historical baseline with ongoing activity indicators**

A useful baseline comes from the 2018 accounting attached to the Brazilian Sign Language Wikipedia project on Meta-Wiki.

That document reported:

- **21 schools and universities**
- across **13 Brazilian states**
- with **over 1,000 students**

The safest use of this figure is as a **documented floor**.

It should not be presented as a complete national census.

It should be presented as the minimum institutional density already visible in that dated project record.

That is already enough to matter.

It also sits inside a longer visible trajectory.

The public record on SignWriting.org traces Brazilian activity back to the 1990s and later connects it to SignNet, UFSC, online courses, school-based literacy work, and continuing symposium activity.

The 2018 floor should not be silently converted into a 2026 present-tense count.

Some institutions may have expanded their work. Some may have reduced or ended it. The present brief uses the 2018 figure as evidence of demonstrated institutional breadth, while keeping current program-by-program status as a documentation need.

## Why the institutional shape matters

The documented network included both Deaf schools and universities.

This is one reason the Brazil case is so important.

It was not merely one classroom or one short-lived pilot site.

The 2018 documentation named university and school settings including:

- the SignWriting Research Group at the Federal University of Santa Catarina
- the Federal University of Ceará in partnership with Deaf-school work in Fortaleza
- the Federal University of Maranhão in São Luís
- Deaf-school use in places such as Santa Maria and Ipiaú

That institutional spread suggests a broader ecosystem of teaching, training, and research.

It also aligns with a second layer of evidence from the symposium archive.

A 2014 SignWriting Symposium presentation by Marianne Rossi Stumpf and Madson Barreto on Brazilian Deaf education reported that since 2006 SignWriting had formed a 180-hour part of the Letras Libras curriculum at UFSC, and that the first two distance offerings in 2006 and 2008 reached more than 1,200 deaf and hearing students across 17 Brazilian states plus the Federal District.

That claim should still be read historically and in context, but it shows that Brazil's institutional role is deeper than a handful of isolated workshops.

## What this means scientifically

Brazil matters scientifically because it provides what many future SignWriting studies will need:

- actual learner populations
- actual educational settings
- repeated institutional environments
- a base for corpora, teaching materials, and longitudinal observation

This does not by itself prove every educational or cognitive claim one might want to make.

It does something more important at this stage:

it makes the research program realistic.

Brazil gives SignWriting research its strongest documented empirical center rather than a purely theoretical future.

The symposium archive reinforces this point.

Brazilian presentations have addressed reading, literacy, online teaching, phonological transcription, bilingual schooling, parallel corpora, television and public communication, school-based implementation, and curricular integration.

That range suggests not only use, but a real research and practice ecosystem.

By 2024, the public record also includes studies of Deaf academic readers' linguistic comfort and cultural identification when reading Libras texts in SignWriting, along with curricular and literary presentations that make the Brazilian case look increasingly mature rather than experimental.

Visible continuity also extends beyond the 2024 symposium layer. Public teaching notices, new teaching-material editions, and ongoing Brazilian publication activity in 2025 suggest that SignWriting remains live in instructional and scholarly settings, even though a fresh nationwide count is still missing.

## Documented reader perspective

The Brazil record is not only institutional.

One useful 2024 presentation, *Leitura de Textos em Libras (SignWriting) por Surdos Acadêmicos: Conforto Linguístico e Identificação Cultural*, explicitly studied Deaf academic readers' perceptions of reading Libras texts in SignWriting.

The abstract describes a qualitative-quantitative study using semi-structured interviews with eight Deaf academics from federal institutions where SignWriting appears in the curriculum.

That does not make the study a population-level outcome result.

It does provide something this evidence series needs: documented reader-centered evidence, including questions of linguistic comfort, identification, perceived comprehension, and obstacles to adherence.

The strongest use of this source is modest:

> Brazil contains not only institutional SignWriting activity, but also documented research asking Deaf readers how written Libras in SignWriting is experienced.

## Adoption limits and resistance

The Brazil case should not be flattened into a success-only story.

The evidence base still has important gaps:

- the current status of each 2018 school or university has not been verified in this brief
- the public record is stronger for institutions, publications, and symposium activity than for ordinary daily use
- documented reader studies remain small
- obstacles to adherence are part of the research record and should be investigated rather than hidden
- some broader public discussions still object to the premise of signed-language Wikipedia or signed-language writing

For example, the Brazilian Sign Language Wikipedia request includes supportive arguments and at least one explicit objection that sign languages are not suited to being written down.

That objection does not outweigh the documented evidence of use.

It does show that adoption evidence should include resistance, not only favorable examples.

## Limits of the claim

The claim here is intentionally narrower than a full national history.

It does **not** claim:

- that the 2018 accounting is complete
- that the 2018 accounting proves the same programs remain active at the same scale today
- that all current Brazilian programs have been verified one by one
- that every degree pathway or curriculum requirement involving SignWriting has already been mapped
- that the Brazilian record alone settles every literacy question
- that all Deaf readers, teachers, or institutions in Brazil hold the same view of SignWriting

A full historical and institutional account of SignWriting in Brazil would be much longer than this account.

But that does not weaken the present point.

The documented floor is already large enough to justify serious attention.

## Documentation needs

The strongest evidence-facing next steps are documentation tasks:

- produce an updated national accounting
- verify current institutional use program by program where needed
- synthesize Brazilian research for English-language audiences
- document reader and teacher perspectives more fully
- identify stalled, dormant, resistant, or discontinued cases alongside successful ones
- distinguish archival activity, curricular requirement, classroom use, publication, and ordinary writing practice

Brazil therefore deserves to be treated not merely as a successful chapter in SignWriting history, but as the strongest documented empirical center in the current public record.
