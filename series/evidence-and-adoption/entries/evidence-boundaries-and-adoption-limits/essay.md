# Evidence Boundaries and Adoption Limits

**What current evidence supports, what remains open, and what resistance still needs documentation**

This entry belongs in *Sutton SignWriting Evidence and Adoption* because evidence discipline should not be buried as a footnote to positive cases.

It answers a skeptical empirical question:

> what does current public evidence make reasonable to claim, and what remains open?

It also answers a second question:

> where are adoption limits, resistance, stalled cases, and negative evidence still underdocumented?

This entry is not yet a complete negative-case catalog.

It is the series boundary statement: the place where supportable claims, open questions, and known documentation gaps are kept visible.

## What current evidence supports

Current public evidence supports a bounded but important conclusion:

> SignWriting is a real writing system with documented educational, scholarly, technical, and institutional use.

That is stronger than a hypothetical proposal.

It supports claims about:

- feasibility
- sustained use in documented settings
- technical processability
- institutional visibility in some contexts
- research relevance
- the need for better evidence infrastructure

The strongest current anchors are different in kind.

Brazil is the strongest documented empirical center because it has visible depth across education, research, publication, and institutional activity.

South Korea is a current institutional proof point because SignWriting appears inside public dictionary infrastructure connected to a national-language institution.

Those two cases should be read together, but not collapsed into one story.

## What current evidence does not yet prove

Current public evidence does not yet prove:

- global adoption scale
- broad population-level literacy outcomes
- uniform effectiveness across educational settings
- that one national case generalizes to all signed-language communities
- that SignWriting should replace video, bilingual literacy, gloss, or research transcription
- that all Deaf communities want the same writing future

These limits do not make the evidence weak.

They define what kind of evidence it is.

## Brazil: strong anchor, dated floor

Brazil remains the strongest documented empirical anchor.

That strength is also a limitation:

- one strong national center is not a global census
- concentrated evidence should not be universalized too quickly
- the public evidence base needs more country-level documentation
- the 2018 institutional floor is a historical baseline, not a current census

The right claim is therefore:

> Brazil shows that SignWriting is empirically real and institutionally visible in at least one strong setting; it does not by itself settle global scale or outcome questions.

The 2018 floor of 21 schools and universities across 13 Brazilian states with more than 1,000 students should travel with its date.

It demonstrates documented institutional breadth at that time.

It does not verify, by itself, which programs remain active at the same scale today.

## South Korea: current proof point, narrow claim

South Korea functions differently.

It matters because it shows SignWriting inside current public language-infrastructure activity.

It should be tracked for:

- maintenance
- expansion
- public use
- educational integration
- long-term institutional continuity

The South Korea claim should remain narrow:

> SignWriting appears in a current public dictionary environment connected to national language infrastructure.

That is important.

It is not the same as proving broad classroom adoption, ordinary daily writing, or long-term national continuity.

## Adoption limits and resistance

The current evidence base is stronger on successful, visible, and institutionally documented cases than on stalled, resistant, discontinued, or contested cases.

That is a limitation of the evidence base.

A serious evidence series should document:

- cases where SignWriting instruction was tried and did not continue
- communities or institutions that preferred other approaches
- reasons teachers, readers, or learners found SignWriting difficult
- documented resistance to signed-language writing in public or academic settings
- adoption paths that remained small despite initial interest
- cases where SignWriting was visible in a tool or curriculum but did not become ordinary practice

At present, the clearest named resistance example in this series is the Brazilian Sign Language Wikipedia request, which included supportive arguments and at least one explicit objection that sign languages are not suited to being written down.

That objection does not outweigh the documented evidence of use.

It does show that adoption evidence should include resistance, not only favorable examples.

## Reader and teacher evidence

The evidence base needs more reader- and teacher-centered documentation.

Current public evidence includes promising but limited work, such as the 2024 Brazilian study of Deaf academic readers' linguistic comfort and cultural identification when reading Libras texts in SignWriting.

That kind of evidence matters because adoption is not only institutional.

It depends on readers, teachers, learners, editors, families, and communities deciding that written signed language is useful, readable, teachable, or worth continuing.

Future evidence should separate:

- institutional adoption
- classroom exposure
- curricular requirement
- reader comfort
- teacher practice
- ordinary writing habits
- publication and dictionary use

Those are related, but they are not the same evidence category.

## Open evidence questions

The next evidence-building work should ask:

- where else is SignWriting being used in sustained ways?
- which uses are educational, scholarly, technical, community-facing, or institutional?
- what forms of use are public and documentable?
- what outcomes have actually been studied?
- which 2018 Brazil institutions remain active, changed, dormant, or discontinued?
- what objections or practical barriers do readers, teachers, and institutions report?
- what claims should remain local, provisional, or exploratory?

The goal is not to make the evidence sound larger than it is.

The goal is to make it easier to build from what is already real.
