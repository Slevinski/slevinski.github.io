# What the Current Evidence Base Shows

**Strong evidence, partial evidence, and how to read SignWriting adoption claims**

This brief gives *Sutton SignWriting Evidence and Adoption* its synthesis layer.

It states, as plainly as possible, what the current public evidence base supports, where the strongest cases sit, and what future evidence-building should target.

The broad conclusion is bounded but important:

> SignWriting is no longer a hypothetical idea without real uptake, but neither is the public evidence base yet a complete worldwide census or a finished large-scale outcomes literature.

That is the right level of confidence.

## What the evidence is already strong on

Several claims are strong enough to state clearly.

### 1. SignWriting is real in practice, not only in theory

The public record already shows that SignWriting exists as:

- a real writing system in use by actual communities
- a real topic of sustained research
- a real technical and standards ecosystem
- a real educational and institutional practice in at least some settings

The question is no longer whether anyone has ever used it.

The question is how the strongest current evidence should be documented, interpreted, and extended.

### 2. Brazil is the strongest documented empirical center

Brazil is currently the strongest documented case for:

- institutional density
- educational continuity
- university involvement
- curricular use
- research activity
- visible long-term public record

Brazil is the clearest current answer to the question:

> where is the strongest documented SignWriting ecosystem?

Here, "current" means the public evidence base available for this release, not a live census.

Brazil is the strongest current case in that public evidence base. Some of the strongest quantitative figures are dated snapshots. The 2018 documented institutional floor shows demonstrated breadth at that time; current activity at the same scale remains a separate source-current question.

### 3. South Korea is a current institutional evidence point

South Korea does a different but equally useful job.

It shows that SignWriting can appear inside:

- official dictionary infrastructure
- corpus-based language work
- public language-institution publication

That makes South Korea especially important when the question is not broad educational density but present-tense institutional legitimacy.

Because the dictionary launch is recent, the Korea case should be treated as evidence of current institutional inclusion, not yet as evidence of long-term sustained adoption.

### 4. The technical and scholarly record is already substantial

The evidence base is not only social or educational.

The record also includes:

- a long research archive
- recurring symposium activity across multiple countries
- dissertations, theses, and publications
- formal encoding systems
- software tools, datasets, and standards work

That matters because it shows that SignWriting is not only a script idea.

It already has the traits of a real scholarly and technical ecosystem.

### 5. Reader-centered evidence exists, but remains limited

The evidence base is not only institutional.

The 2024 Brazil presentation on Deaf academic readers' linguistic comfort and cultural identification shows that at least some research is asking how Deaf readers experience written Libras in SignWriting.

That matters because adoption is not only a matter of schools, dictionaries, and publication counts.

It is also a matter of reader experience, comfort, obstacles, identification, and ordinary use.

The evidence here is promising but still small. It should be treated as a starting point for reader-centered documentation, not as a completed community reception study.

## What still needs better evidence

Some questions need stronger documentation before they can carry broad claims.

### 1. There is no complete current global map

The public record does not yet provide:

- a full worldwide census of active SignWriting users
- a verified program-by-program international institutional map
- a stable present-tense accounting of where use is strong, weak, emerging, or dormant

That makes updated country and regional mapping an important next task.

### 2. Large-scale literacy outcome claims remain limited

Large-scale literacy outcome claims need stronger study designs than the current public record provides.

Future work should distinguish:

- national-scale superiority claims
- general claims about signed-language literacy
- cross-context educational outcome claims

The stronger current claim is:

- SignWriting has documented real use, real promise, and a research space worth taking seriously

### 3. Community desire cannot be assumed from limited cases

Community reception remains an empirical question. The evidence should distinguish:

- documented use
- documented interest
- documented resistance
- local preference
- bilingual literacy concerns
- transfer from one country case to another

The current record supports real use, real institutional adoption, and real literacy interest in some places. Broader claims need broader evidence.

### 4. Adoption limits and negative cases are underdocumented

The public evidence base is stronger on successful, visible, and institutionally documented cases than on stalled, resistant, discontinued, or contested cases.

That gap points to a useful next evidence agenda:

- cases where SignWriting instruction was tried and did not continue
- communities or institutions that preferred other approaches
- reasons teachers, readers, or learners found SignWriting difficult
- documented resistance to signed-language writing in public or academic settings
- adoption paths that remained small despite initial interest

The purpose is to make the evidence more complete and more useful.

That documentation belongs in *Evidence Boundaries and Adoption Limits*.

### 5. Comparative adoption evidence remains incomplete

Comparative adoption evidence between SignWriting, ASLwrite, HamNoSys, ASL-phabet, or other signed-language writing and notation systems remains incomplete.

The available record suggests that SignWriting has unusually broad documented educational, technical, and international infrastructure compared with many alternatives.

The strongest future comparison would document the adoption record for alternative systems with the same care.

## How the evidence should be read

The best way to read the current evidence base is through three levels.

### Level 1: Existence

The weakest skeptical question is:

> is this merely an idea?

No. Brazil, South Korea, the research archive, and the technical record are already enough to answer that.

### Level 2: Strength of current anchors

The next question is:

> where is the evidence strongest?

The current answer is:

- Brazil for depth, continuity, and educational-research density
- South Korea for current institutional evidence

### Level 3: What still needs better documentation

The next serious question is:

> what would make the evidence base more complete and more persuasive?

The answer includes:

- updated institutional mapping
- better English-language synthesis of Brazil and Korea materials
- clearer lineage and tradition documentation
- better present-tense accounting of active programs and materials
- better documentation of stalled, resistant, and discontinued cases
- better documentation of Deaf reader and teacher perspectives
- comparative adoption mapping across alternative scripts and notation systems
- later outcome studies built on real existing users and texts

## What can already be claimed responsibly

The current public evidence supports the following claims:

- SignWriting is already real in educational, research, and technical practice
- Brazil is the strongest currently documented empirical center
- South Korea is one of the clearest current institutional evidence points
- the SignWriting ecosystem already includes meaningful scholarly and technical continuity
- some reader-centered evidence exists, especially in the Brazilian record
- the work should be treated as real infrastructure and real research material rather than as a merely speculative possibility

## Claims reserved for future evidence

Future evidence work should be designed before making:

- a complete global adoption map
- sweeping worldwide population estimates
- large-scale superiority claims across all literacy contexts
- assumptions that every community wants the same writing future
- claims that dated institutional floors prove present-tense activity everywhere they once documented
- claims that SignWriting's adoption has already been systematically compared against all alternative systems

Keeping those claims for later lets the current evidence stand on firmer ground.
