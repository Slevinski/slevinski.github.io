# Country Ambassadors

**Country-level outreach, translation, and local relationship-building**

Country ambassadors give Sutton SignWriting a practical way to build international relationships without pretending that one central team can understand every local context.

Its core claim is simple:

> international support needs trusted local relationship capacity

This entry defines a volunteer-first country ambassador, or local liaison, model for outreach, translation, documentation, and early partner development.

## Why The Role Is Needed

International outreach is difficult to centralize well.

Each country may have different:

- university and school structures
- languages and communication norms
- economic conditions and funding realities
- Deaf education networks
- SignWriting communities and leaders
- institutional pathways for research, gifts, grants, or collaboration

A country ambassador model creates local relationship capacity without asking one central team to understand every country on its own.

## Role Summary

A country ambassador, also describable in formal settings as a local liaison, is a trusted local volunteer who helps connect Sutton SignWriting with educators, researchers, universities, schools, community leaders, and other useful contacts in a specific country.

The role begins as volunteer service.

If funding and administrative pathways become clear, some ambassador work may later become stipend-supported service work for defined tasks such as translation, outreach coordination, documentation, or meeting support.

The role should stay practical.

It is a relationship, communication, and local-context role, not a prestige title.

The title should never imply that one person represents an entire country, Deaf community, institution, or SignWriting user base.

## Core Responsibilities

Country ambassadors may help:

- identify universities, schools, programs, and community organizations
- identify faculty, administrators, educators, and local champions
- make outreach more culturally and linguistically appropriate
- translate or adapt short outreach materials
- make introductions and support early conversations
- explain local conditions, constraints, and priorities
- gather statements of use, letters of support, and collaboration interest
- document visible SignWriting use and impact in the country
- help the central team understand which opportunities are realistic

The most valuable work is often simple:

- who should be contacted?
- what should be said?
- what local history matters?
- what follow-up is respectful?
- which claims would overstate the local situation?

## Role Boundaries

Country ambassadors are not expected to:

- make legal commitments on behalf of Sutton SignWriting
- make financial commitments on behalf of the project
- negotiate institutional agreements independently
- serve as payroll or payment intermediaries
- represent any host institution or partner organization in a formal administrative capacity unless explicitly authorized
- speak for all Deaf people, all SignWriting users, or all institutions in a country

These boundaries protect the ambassadors, the project, and the institutions involved.

The role is relational and informational.

Formal authority should remain with the appropriate institutions and authorized project leads.

## Role Agreement

A lightweight role agreement should make expectations explicit before the pilot grows.

It should include:

- respectful local communication
- no unauthorized legal, financial, institutional, or public commitments
- confidentiality when requested by partners or participants
- accurate documentation of contacts, needs, and follow-up
- permission before making public claims about local support or institutional interest
- a clear reporting path to the central project contact
- disclosure of conflicts of interest or paid roles that could affect outreach

This agreement does not need to be heavy or legalistic.

Its purpose is to protect trust while keeping the role practical.

## Volunteer-First Pilot

The first version should be small and clear.

A practical pilot would include:

- three to five countries where active use or institutional interest is already visible
- a short ambassador description
- a simple onboarding note
- shared outreach templates that can be localized
- a shared contact and follow-up tracker
- a clear contact person in the central project
- visible recognition of ambassador contributions

The pilot should be designed to learn.

It should answer questions such as:

- which materials are useful?
- which contacts respond?
- which translation needs are real?
- which support letters are possible?
- which countries need different outreach patterns?
- what administrative support would be needed before stipends are introduced?

## Path To Stipends

If institutional support grows, some ambassador work may become stipend-supported.

Possible stipend-supported services include:

- translation or localization
- outreach coordination
- meeting facilitation
- event support
- documentation of local use cases
- preparation of partner statements or local summaries

Stipends should be tied to defined services, not vague status.

They should also account for the fact that economic conditions differ widely across countries. A responsible model will need administrative clarity, fair expectations, and country-sensitive judgment.

## Selection Criteria

Strong ambassadors are likely to have some combination of:

- trusted standing in local SignWriting or Deaf education communities
- strong communication skills
- familiarity with universities, schools, or institutional networks
- ability to work across language and cultural boundaries
- reliability in documentation and follow-up
- a practical understanding of local opportunities and constraints

No one person needs to be perfect.

The first priority is trustworthiness and follow-through.

## Initial Countries

A practical first cohort could include:

- Brazil
- Mexico
- South Korea
- additional countries where active SignWriting use or institutional interest is already visible

The first cohort should stay small enough to support well.

Expansion should happen after the model has been tested.

## Relationship To International Shared Support

Country ambassadors are one mechanism inside the larger international support model.

They help make international participation practical by grounding outreach in local knowledge.

They should not replace institutional partnerships, formal agreements, or central project stewardship.

They help those things become possible.

## Recommended Next Step

Recruit a small pilot group in three to five countries.

Give them simple materials, clear boundaries, and a shared follow-up process.

Then learn from the pilot before expanding.
