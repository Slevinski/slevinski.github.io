# Infrastructure Core and Philanthropy

**A stewardship model for staffing, translation, coordination, and continuity**

Sutton SignWriting requires grant-ready research pathways.

It also requires continuity capacity that grants alone often do not provide.

That is where an infrastructure core and philanthropy become useful.

The core idea is simple:

> create a small, focused stewardship structure around existing SignWriting standards, software, data, public resources, translation needs, and international coordination

This is not a proposal to invent a new system.

It is a proposal to stabilize, document, and extend infrastructure that already exists.

## Why an infrastructure core is needed

The current SignWriting ecosystem includes real assets:

- standards
- fonts
- software libraries
- public websites
- dictionaries and datasets
- written materials
- international user relationships

The problem is that these assets remain too dependent on informal maintenance and limited continuity capacity.

An infrastructure core would make the stewardship function explicit.

It would give the work a place to hold:

- technical stewardship
- documentation
- data preservation
- partner coordination
- translation and access
- research readiness
- continuity planning

## What the core would do

A small infrastructure core could support:

| Function | Practical work |
| --- | --- |
| Standards maintenance | Keep ISWA, FSW, SWU, fonts, and related documentation coherent |
| Software stewardship | Maintain libraries, tools, services, and Platform V3 development |
| Data stewardship | Preserve dictionaries, corpora, metadata, and migration pathways |
| Translation and access | Identify high-value works, secure permissions, and support reviewed translations |
| Global coordination | Support partners, local liaisons, statements of use, and follow-up |
| Research integration | Help convert infrastructure into usable study materials, corpora, and grants |
| Succession planning | Reduce single-maintainer dependency through documentation and shared capacity |

This is a high-leverage stewardship layer.

It is intentionally smaller than a large center.

## Translation and access

Translation and access deserve special attention.

Some important SignWriting materials already exist in Portuguese and other languages but remain difficult for English-centric academic audiences to use.

A disciplined translation and access program could:

- identify priority works
- secure permission from authors, publishers, or rights holders
- use AI-assisted translation only as a draft-support tool
- require human editorial review
- return finished translations in forms approved by the original authors or communities
- support online hosting, print, or co-publication where appropriate

This should not become extractive translation.

The goal is to make important work more accessible while respecting authors, communities, rights, and local context.

The program should translate selectively, beginning with the most important and most permission-ready materials.

## Why philanthropy fits

Philanthropy fits because SignWriting infrastructure sits between usual funding categories.

It is:

- too technical for many humanities-only funding routes
- too language- and community-centered for purely technical funding
- too long-term for short grant cycles alone
- too operational for research grants to carry comfortably by themselves

Philanthropy can support the continuity layer that grants often underfund:

- staffing
- documentation
- maintenance
- translation
- coordination
- bridge funding
- match support for grants

That does not make philanthropy a replacement for grants.

It makes philanthropy a stabilizing partner.

## Gift and continuity scenarios

Exact budgets should be developed with a host institution or fiscal sponsor.

Still, the continuity tiers can be described generically.

| Tier | What it supports | Result |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Continuity capacity | Hosting, backups, documentation, domain continuity | Reduces immediate collapse risk |
| Stabilization capacity | Part-time technical help, data preservation, platform maintenance | Makes the infrastructure easier to sustain |
| Program capacity | Core staff time, translation/access work, partner coordination | Turns stewardship into a visible program |
| Growth capacity | Grant matching, student/research assistant support, Platform V3 acceleration | Makes broader research and collaboration more realistic |

A large philanthropic gift could combine several tiers.

Smaller gifts can still be useful if they are tied to concrete continuity or access tasks.

## Relationship to grants

The strongest funding model is layered.

Grants are strongest for:

- research studies
- corpus development
- computational methods
- literacy and reading research
- accessibility and education projects
- platform development tied to research aims

Philanthropy is strongest for:

- bridge staffing
- translation and access
- continuity and maintenance
- planning time before grants
- unfunded infrastructure follow-through
- partner coordination that does not fit neatly into one grant

The two routes should reinforce each other.

Philanthropy can make grants more credible by keeping the infrastructure stable enough for researchers to build on it.

Grants can make philanthropy more credible by showing concrete research outcomes.

## What should stay modest

The infrastructure-core idea should not be oversold.

It should not promise:

- universal adoption
- immediate literacy outcomes
- instant institutional maturity
- full translation of all important materials
- one permanent solution to all maintenance needs

Its first promise is narrower:

> make the existing infrastructure more stable and more usable for research, education, preservation, and international collaboration

That is enough.

## Bottom line

Sutton SignWriting requires research funding, and it also requires the stewardship layer that makes research funding usable.

An infrastructure core gives that layer a name.

Philanthropy gives it one realistic continuity path.

Together, they can turn thin continuity into a stronger base for standards, software, data, translation, access, and international collaboration.
