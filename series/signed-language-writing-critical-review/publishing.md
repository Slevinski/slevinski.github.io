## Series Description

This series applies a layer-based critique framework to signed-language writing scholarship, separating writing, notation, encoding, rendering, and text structure so that claims are judged at the level where they actually belong.

Critical review series on signed-language writing, SignWriting, notation, encoding, standards, grapholinguistics, and durable signed text infrastructure.

## Artifact Metadata

### A Unified Critique Framework

- Type: Working Framework
- DOI posture: separate artifact DOI planned
- Source path: `entries/a-unified-critique-framework-for-signed-language-writing-systems/essay.md`

Abstract:

> This framework provides a reusable method for evaluating signed language writing systems, encodings, standards, and representation proposals without collapsing distinct layers into a single judgment. It separates language, writing system, notation, semantic representation, encoding, rendering, empirical grounding, and institutional infrastructure, then asks what each source actually claims to solve. The framework is especially useful for assessing SignWriting-related literature because SignWriting exposes recurring confusions between symbols and written signs, serialization and writing, encoding and rendering, and technical feasibility and durable text infrastructure. Its purpose is not to dismiss alternative approaches, but to preserve useful insights while identifying level confusion, overreach, and missing infrastructure requirements.

### Sutton SignWriting and Formal SignWriting Through the Framework

- Type: Working Paper
- DOI posture: separate artifact DOI planned
- Source path: `entries/sutton-signwriting-and-formal-signwriting-through-the-framework/essay.md`

Abstract:

> This framework application evaluates Sutton SignWriting and Formal SignWriting with the same criteria used elsewhere in the critical review series. It distinguishes SignWriting as the human writing system from Formal SignWriting as the technical text model that preserves written signs for storage, search, processing, and rendering. The analysis concludes that the combined system is strongest where the framework asks about durable signed text, structured written units, and infrastructure viability, while remaining careful about adoption, literacy outcomes, handwriting practice, and unresolved standards questions. Its central claim is disciplined: SignWriting and Formal SignWriting establish a coherent full-path model for plane-based signed writing, but they do not settle every educational, empirical, or institutional question by themselves.

### A Layer-Based Evaluation of Signed Language Writing Systems

- Type: Working Paper
- DOI posture: separate artifact DOI planned
- Source path: `entries/a-layer-based-evaluation-of-signed-language-writing-systems/essay.md`
- Companion: `entries/a-layer-based-evaluation-of-signed-language-writing-systems/comparative-matrix.md`

Abstract:

> This comparative paper applies the critical review framework to four representative cases: Filhol (2020), Grushkin (2017), Thiessen (2011), and Unicode SignWriting. The comparison does not treat these cases as a complete survey of signed language writing research. Instead, it uses them as a bounded demonstration of a recurring pattern: each work identifies a real part of the signed-writing problem, but the insight is often displaced onto the wrong layer or pressed too far toward becoming the whole answer. The paper argues that signed writing analysis becomes clearer when semantic representation, literacy design, symbol analysis, character standardization, writing systems, text models, and rendering are kept distinct. Its conclusion is methodological as much as evaluative: the field needs layer discipline in order to preserve valid insights without mistaking them for complete solutions.
