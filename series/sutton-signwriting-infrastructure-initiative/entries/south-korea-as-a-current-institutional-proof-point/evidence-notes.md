# South Korea as a Current Institutional Proof Point Evidence Notes

**South Korea as a Current Institutional Proof Point**  
National dictionary publication, current institutional use, and why Korea matters now

These evidence notes record the narrow factual basis for the Korea brief.

The claim is intentionally narrower than the Brazil brief.

It is not that South Korea is the largest or deepest documented SignWriting ecosystem.

It is that South Korea provides a current institutional proof point: SignWriting appears inside a public Korean Sign Language dictionary associated with the National Institute of Korean Language.

## Core factual basis

The central public fact is the opening of the Korean Sign Language Nuri Dictionary on **February 6, 2025**.

The project record and launch materials describe the dictionary as:

- based on Korean Sign Language corpus data
- publicly searchable
- inclusive of Korean equivalent expressions and example information
- inclusive of sign-form pictures
- inclusive of sign-language characters
- shaped with Deaf participation in the compilation process

For the purposes of this brief, the strongest use of those facts is:

> South Korea provides current public institutional evidence that SignWriting can function inside dictionary infrastructure.

That formulation is strong enough without overstating the case.

## Why the Korea case matters

The Korea case is strategically useful because it is:

- recent
- public
- institutional
- lexicographic
- visible in interface form rather than only in private documentation

This makes it different from a historical anecdote or a private software trial.

It also makes it different from the Brazil case.

Brazil provides stronger evidence of institutional density, educational spread, and research depth.

South Korea provides stronger evidence of current official public dictionary presence.

## What appears to be present in the dictionary workflow

The public descriptions emphasize features that matter for infrastructure interpretation:

- sign-form search
- Korean search
- sign-language video menus and instructions
- sign-language characters
- customizable screen display

These features matter because they show that the work is not purely symbolic.

They point to a real operational workflow involving language data, interface choices, visual representation, and public access.

Project notes associated with the Korean work further indicate that the signs were prepared in SignWriting through SignPuddle Online.

That point is especially important for infrastructure planning because it suggests that current Korean institutional work is tied to existing SignWriting tooling rather than to a separate isolated implementation.

## What the Korean launch reveals about infrastructure dependence

The Korea case is not only important because of what appeared in public.

It is also important because of what the launch reportedly required operationally.

Project notes indicate that the Korean team depended on SignPuddle 2 on the legacy SignWriting server to author signs for the dictionary.

Under launch pressure, large-scale use appears to have exposed scaling limits in the older system, especially around slower save behavior and overlapping save operations.

Direct support was needed during that period, including two days of vacation time taken to keep the launch effort moving.

The Korean team reportedly split into morning and evening groups to keep progress moving around those constraints.

That operational story matters because it shows that the Korea case was not a symbolic endorsement resting on separate local tools.

It depended on live SignWriting infrastructure that remains useful but fragile.

## Broader Korean context already visible in the public record

The current Korea case does not stand entirely alone.

The public SignWriting record also includes:

- the SignWriting in Korea country page on SignWriting.org
- Jongtae Kim's 2024 SignWriting Symposium presentation
- Jongtae Kim's 2025 book on Korean and sign language, presented on the Korea page with Valerie Sutton's commentary

These materials strengthen the interpretation that Korean SignWriting work is not accidental or momentary.

They do not need to carry the whole argument.

The public dictionary itself already carries the central institutional point.

## What should still be said carefully

Several stronger claims would still require additional verification.

This brief does **not** establish:

- the full extent of present educational use across South Korea
- a complete institutional map of Korean SignWriting use
- nationwide everyday writing practices
- the long-term stability of the current institutional workflow without support

Those are future research and documentation tasks.

They are not prerequisites for the narrower present claim.

## Bottom line

South Korea should not be treated as the broadest SignWriting case.

It should be treated as one of the clearest current institutional proof points.

That is already enough to matter for infrastructure, partnership, and stewardship planning.
