# Unicode Editorial Rules

**House rules for the hidden Unicode section**

These rules keep the Unicode lane readable, disciplined, and reusable across different audiences.

## 1. Compatibility before grievance

Lead with:

- what works
- what breaks
- what is incompatible

Do not lead with personal frustration, institutional disappointment, or past conflict.

Those may belong in private notes.

They do not belong at the center of the public Unicode library.

## 2. One document, one main job

Each document should have one main job:

- orient
- guide developers
- document chronology
- brief Wikimedia
- address UTC

Do not let one document drift into doing all five jobs.

## 3. Symbol block is not the same as writing support

Keep this distinction visible early and often:

- encoding a symbol inventory
- supporting written signs in practice

This is the central editorial distinction of the whole library.

## 4. Use strong judgment, but careful wording

The tone should be:

- calm
- exact
- confident
- non-defensive

Prefer:

- not compatible
- insufficient for production use
- unresolved
- lacks a workable path

Avoid unnecessary heat in outward-facing documents.

## 5. Separate fact, judgment, and request

Whenever possible, keep these distinct:

- factual record
- technical judgment
- requested next step

This makes the documents easier to trust and easier to reuse.

## 6. Keep the ecosystem visible

Do not let Unicode discussion become too abstract.

Anchor it in real things:

- FSW
- SWU
- ISWA 2010
- datasets
- fonts
- dictionaries
- published materials
- Brazil
- South Korea
- package and tooling reality

## 7. Do not treat every Unicode-oriented artifact as endorsement

Packages such as `@sutton-signwriting/unicode8` should be described clearly:

- bridge tooling
- compatibility tooling
- processing tooling

Their existence does not change the broader compatibility judgment.

## 8. The next step should always be visible

A reader should be able to tell what follows from each document:

- read a shorter overview
- inspect the chronology
- use the developer note
- prepare for a Wikimedia meeting
- move into the UTC note
- consider whether renewed Unicode work is worth pursuing

## 9. Avoid repetitive scaffolding phrases

Watch for repeated patterns such as:

- that matters because
- that is important because
- the current position is
- the current practical position is

Use them when they help.

Cut them when they begin to sound mechanical.

## 10. Preserve room for future engagement

The documents may judge the current official Unicode path strongly.

They should still leave room for:

- renewed discussion
- support from a voting member
- better technical work in the future

The position is firm.

It should not sound closed to honest technical progress.
