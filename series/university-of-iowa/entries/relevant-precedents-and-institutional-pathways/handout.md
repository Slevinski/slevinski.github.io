# Relevant Precedents and Institutional Pathways

**What comparable university models suggest for SignWriting at Iowa**

The SignWriting proposal follows a recognizable institutional pattern rather than asking Iowa to invent an entirely new kind of project.

Across language and data infrastructure, the same pattern appears repeatedly:

> a technically mature language or data resource already exists -> a university provides an immediate credible home, often under a single PI or institute -> that home supplies shared technical capacity and grant-writing leverage -> the resource scales into durable, grant-ready infrastructure

None of these examples began by inventing a system from scratch.

All began with what SignWriting already has:

- working data
- tools
- standards
- technical stewardship
- real community or research use

## Why precedents matter

The point is not that SignWriting is identical to any one example.

The point is that the Iowa path is recognizable:

- mature assets first
- institutional anchoring second
- practical capacity and credibility next
- grants, philanthropy, and longer-term infrastructure growth after that

## Closest analogs

These are the most relevant examples because they show university-housed data or language infrastructure that started with existing assets and grew through institutional anchoring.

### 1. ICPSR, University of Michigan

- **Founding year**: 1962
- **How it started**: Political scientist Warren E. Miller created it at the University of Michigan's Institute for Social Research as the data-dissemination arm of the American National Election Studies, at a time when important datasets were still closely held by individual researchers.
- **Funding model**: It began with membership dues from 21 founding universities and later expanded through NSF support, Ford Foundation support, and growing consortial membership.
- **What it provides**: Long-term preservation, curation, documentation, training, researcher services, and public access to a very large social-science data archive.
- **Why relevant to SignWriting**: This is a classic example of a university giving a durable home to an already-functioning shared-data need and turning it into globally recognized research infrastructure.
- **Sources**:
  - https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/sites/icpsr/about/history
  - https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/sites/icpsr/about/history/early-years

### 2. ELDP / ELAR

- **Founding year**: 2002
- **How it started**: The Endangered Languages Documentation Programme and Endangered Languages Archive were launched through a major Arcadia Foundation donation to SOAS, paired with a model in which funded documentation work was archived as part of the program itself.
- **Funding model**: Philanthropic seed funding, annual documentation grants, and sustained institutional hosting.
- **What it provides**: Grants for language documentation plus a permanent multimedia archive that preserves and shares funded materials over time.
- **Why relevant to SignWriting**: It shows that philanthropy can be a strong continuity lane when paired with real institutional anchoring rather than treated as a stand-alone substitute for structure.
- **Sources**:
  - https://www.eldp.net/
  - https://www.eldp.net/en/about+us/who+we+are/

### 3. PARADISEC

- **Founding year**: 2003, with the consortium formalized shortly afterward
- **How it started**: Faculty at multiple Australian universities recognized that endangered-language and cultural recordings were at risk of loss and created a shared preservation and access model.
- **Funding model**: Distributed university support combined with research-infrastructure grants and shared operational responsibility.
- **What it provides**: Long-term archiving, cataloguing, digitization, preservation, and community access for endangered-language and cultural recordings.
- **Why relevant to SignWriting**: It demonstrates that the institutional home can be distributed yet durable, with preservation and public access treated as central infrastructure functions.
- **Sources**:
  - https://www.paradisec.org.au/about-us/
  - https://www.paradisec.org.au/

### 4. BSL Corpus Project -> BSL SignBank, University College London

- **Founding year**: The BSL Corpus Project ran from 2008 to 2011, with SignBank development continuing afterward
- **How it started**: An ESRC-funded sign-language documentation effort at UCL's Deafness, Cognition and Language Research Centre produced a large corpus that fed directly into reusable lexical infrastructure.
- **Funding model**: Major research-council funding followed by continued university hosting and stewardship.
- **What it provides**: A large video corpus together with a public lexical resource used for research, teaching, and community access.
- **Why relevant to SignWriting**: This is one of the closest sign-language-specific precedents because it shows a university-hosted sign-language corpus maturing into durable, reusable lexical infrastructure.
- **Sources**:
  - https://bslsignbank.ucl.ac.uk/
  - https://www.ucl.ac.uk/brain-sciences/pals/research/deafness-cognition-and-language-dcal/research-dcal/linguistics-research

## Adjacent but useful analogs

These examples are not perfect structural matches, but they strengthen important parts of the case.

### 5. ASLLRP / ASL SignBank / ASLLVD, Boston University

- **Founding period**: Late 1990s and early 2000s, with continued expansion
- **How it started**: Linguistic research work collecting and annotating ASL video data developed into public tools and lexical resources.
- **Funding model**: NIH and NSF grants plus university support.
- **What it provides**: Large video corpora, lexical resources, search interfaces, and downloadable annotations.
- **Why relevant to SignWriting**: It shows that sign-language infrastructure naturally converges on the same ecosystem components that SignWriting already has or can support: corpora, lexical databases, annotation, and tools.
- **Sources**:
  - https://www.bu.edu/asllrp/
  - https://www.bu.edu/asllrp/indexright.html

### 6. MIDAS, University of Michigan

- **Founding period**: Around 2015, with its 10th anniversary marked in 2025
- **How it started**: A cross-campus institute was created to advance data science and AI as shared research infrastructure across many disciplines.
- **Funding model**: University support plus internal pilot grants, research services, proposal support, and partnership development.
- **What it provides**: Proposal support, pilot funding, staff-scientist access, collaborator matching, training, and cross-campus research coordination.
- **Why relevant to SignWriting**: This is one of the best models for the immediate step being proposed at Iowa: place Steve as shared technical capacity in an existing lab or institute, create practical value now, and use that role to build proposals and broader infrastructure later.
- **Sources**:
  - https://midas.umich.edu/about/
  - https://midas.umich.edu/research/research-resources/funding-support/
