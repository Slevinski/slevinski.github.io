# Iconicity, Convention, and Legibility

**How motivated visual form becomes disciplined writing**

This paper develops the legibility and convention argument in *Writing and Representation in Sutton SignWriting*.

It follows *Perspective and Authored Space* and prepares the ground for *Handwriting and Spatial Script Practice*.

The earlier papers make three claims:

- writing does not have to be linear
- SignWriting is best described with both a representational axis and an organizational axis
- the writer authors viewpoint, relation, and composed space inside the written sign

This paper asks the next question:

> if SignWriting is visually motivated, why is it writing rather than drawing?

The answer is not that SignWriting has no iconicity.

The answer is the opposite:

> iconicity is not the opposite of writing

Iconicity becomes compatible with writing when motivated visual form is stabilized by convention, contrast, repetition, teachability, correction, and reader practice.

That is the central claim of this paper.

## The common mistake

SignWriting is easy to misread at first glance.

It contains visible traces of bodies, hands, movement, faces, contact, and space.

A hand symbol may resemble a hand.

A movement arrow may suggest a path.

A facial symbol may point toward an expression or body location.

The signbox may look like a small visual scene.

Because of that, some readers make a quick jump:

> if it looks like what it represents, it must be drawing rather than writing

That conclusion is too fast.

The question is not whether a written form has visual motivation.

The question is what kind of system controls that motivation.

Free drawing can invent resemblance locally.

Writing stabilizes form through shared conventions.

The issue is not:

- iconic or arbitrary

The better issue is:

- locally improvised or conventionally disciplined

SignWriting belongs on the second side of that distinction.

## Iconicity is not a disqualification

Many writing systems and notational systems contain traces of motivation.

Some letterforms have historical visual origins.

Some scripts preserve graphic analogy.

Mathematical notation uses spatial and visual relations that are not purely arbitrary.

Musical notation uses vertical placement, repeated symbols, stems, beams, rests, and other visible conventions that are learned as part of a system.

Maps and diagrams use motivated visual relations, although they are not all language writing.

The presence of motivation does not settle the classification.

Motivated form can appear in:

- illustration
- notation
- diagramming
- writing
- interface design
- symbolic art

The classification depends on how the form works.

For writing, the crucial questions are:

- Are forms repeatable?
- Are distinctions shared?
- Are contrasts teachable?
- Can readers learn what counts as the same or different?
- Can a writer be corrected?
- Can texts be copied, stored, compared, searched, and reused?
- Does the system support durable language-bearing text?

Those questions move the discussion away from a shallow opposition between iconicity and writing.

They ask how iconicity is conventionalized.

They also ask what the iconicity is doing.

That question matters because a visible form can be motivated without being language writing.

## Iconicity is not the same as pictography

Writing-systems theory often distinguishes language writing from broader visible meaning-making.

Street signs, pictograms, emoji, interface icons, maps, and diagrams can communicate through visual resemblance or convention.

They can be useful, stable, and widely understood.

But they are not automatically writing in the narrower language-writing sense.

The difference is not that they lack convention.

Many of them are highly conventional.

The difference is that they often point directly to ideas, actions, affect, places, or situations rather than writing a language form as text.

That distinction matters for SignWriting.

SignWriting is not primarily a set of icons for ideas.

It writes signed language forms.

Its symbols are visually motivated by hands, movement, facial expression, body location, and spatial relation, but those motivated symbols are used to compose written signs that belong to signed language.

This keeps the argument from sliding into a weaker claim:

> SignWriting is writing because pictures can communicate

That is not the claim.

The stronger claim is:

> SignWriting is writing because visually motivated symbols are conventionalized into durable language-bearing written forms

Iconicity can help explain why some symbols look the way they do.

It does not by itself explain why the system is writing.

For that, the system must support written identity, contrast, composition, correction, reading, and reuse.

## What convention does

Convention is not decoration placed on top of writing.

Convention is what lets writing become shareable.

In SignWriting, convention turns visually motivated marks into a usable writing system by making forms:

- repeatable
- teachable
- contrastive
- correctable
- comparable
- searchable
- legible across writers
- durable across media

This is why a symbol that resembles a hand is not merely a hand picture.

Inside the system, it is a selected written symbol.

It has identity.

It has contrast with other symbols.

It participates in fill, rotation, placement, movement, and relation conventions.

It can be copied.

It can be encoded.

It can be searched.

It can be corrected when the wrong symbol or relation is used.

Correctable does not mean governed by a single global authority.

It means that within a community, teaching tradition, dictionary, classroom, corpus, or publication context, readers and writers can recognize when a written form works, when it does not, and when another form would be clearer.

That is what convention does.

It does not remove visual motivation.

It disciplines it.

More precisely, convention gives visual motivation a role inside a system.

It answers questions a drawing can leave open:

| Question | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| What counts as the same symbol? | Without symbol identity, forms drift into private drawing. |
| What counts as a contrast? | Without contrast, readers cannot know which differences matter. |
| What counts as a valid placement? | Without placement conventions, spatial relation becomes guesswork. |
| What counts as a complete written sign? | Without boundaries, texts become loose clusters of marks. |
| What can be corrected? | Without correctability, instruction and editing remain unstable. |
| What can be preserved? | Without preservation, text cannot reliably move across media. |

These questions are not merely technical.

They are writing-systems questions.

They describe the path from visual motivation to written form.

## How convention emerges

Convention in SignWriting is not a central authority deciding the one correct written form for every sign.

The same symbol set and encoding model can support many sign languages and many writing traditions.

Writers choose how to write a sign.

Readers decide whether that writing is readable, useful, and worth accepting.

Communities, schools, dictionaries, corpora, and repeated use then shape which written forms become common.

This is bottom-up convention.

A text-entry system may show existing written signs, but those signs are examples available to the writer, not rules imposed on the writer.

The writer may reuse an existing form, adapt it, or write a new form.

Over time, useful forms recur; unclear, awkward, or idiosyncratic forms tend to remain rare.

In this sense, SignWriting behaves like a living writing system.

Good writing is not defined globally by decree.

It emerges through use.

## The SignWriting case

The signed writing system considered here is Sutton SignWriting.

In SignWriting, iconicity and convention meet at multiple layers.

| Layer | Iconicity role | Convention role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hand symbols | motivated by visible hand form | the symbol inventory stabilizes distinctions among handshapes |
| Fill and rotation | motivated by palm facing and orientation | fill and rotation categories make orientation readable and comparable |
| Movement arrows | motivated by path, direction, and movement type | arrow conventions define how motion is represented in writing |
| Facial and body symbols | motivated by visible expression and body location | symbol classes and placement stabilize what is selected for writing |
| Signbox placement | motivated by signed space and body relation | spatial conventions turn placement into written relation |
| Sign boundaries | motivated by the composed sign as a unit | boundaries let signs become text units |
| Passage layout | motivated by reading flow and visual organization | columns, lanes, and orientation stabilize passage reading |

No single layer proves that SignWriting is writing.

The system matters as a whole.

A hand symbol alone is not the writing system.

A movement arrow alone is not the writing system.

A signbox alone is not the writing system.

The writing system emerges from the disciplined relation among symbol inventory, placement, viewpoint, movement, boundaries, passage layout, and reader practice.

That is why SignWriting should not be reduced to either of two oversimplifications:

- an intuitive picture system
- an arbitrary technical code

It is neither.

It is visually motivated writing governed by learned conventions.

## Legibility is learned contrast

Legibility is often misunderstood as immediate visual recognition.

That misunderstanding matters here.

A novice may see a cluster of marks and think:

> this looks like hands and movement

A reader sees something more specific:

- this handshape, not that one
- this palm facing, not that one
- this rotation, not that one
- this movement path, not that one
- this location relation, not that one
- this facial or body symbol, not that one
- this sign boundary, not a loose collection of symbols

Legibility is not raw resemblance.

It is trained recognition inside a system of contrast.

This is true far beyond SignWriting.

Readers of alphabetic scripts do not merely see lines and curves. They see letters, variants, ligatures, punctuation, word boundaries, and acceptable or unacceptable forms.

Readers of musical notation do not merely see dots and stems. They see pitch, duration, grouping, meter, phrasing, and performance instructions.

Readers of mathematical notation do not merely see marks scattered on a page. They see exponentiation, grouping, fraction structure, matrices, variables, operators, and scope.

The same principle applies to SignWriting.

The writing is visible, but its legibility is learned.

Iconicity may help a new reader approach the system.

It does not replace literacy.

This distinction can be stated sharply:

> transparency is not the same as legibility

A transparent form looks motivated to a viewer.

A legible form is recognized within a learned system of written distinctions.

Some SignWriting symbols may be relatively transparent to beginners.

Others may not be.

Even transparent symbols become fully legible only when the reader knows what differences matter.

That is why the system should not be evaluated by asking only what an untrained viewer can guess.

An untrained viewer may guess something useful.

That does not mean the viewer is reading.

Reading begins when visual recognition is disciplined by learned contrast.

## Iconicity and abstraction

It would be a mistake to say that every part of SignWriting is transparent.

Some forms are more visually obvious than others.

Some are highly conventional.

Some require instruction before they become legible.

Some distinctions may be difficult for beginners but important for fluent reading and writing.

This is normal.

Writing systems do not become less real because they contain abstraction.

They also do not become less real because some forms are motivated.

SignWriting sits between two bad descriptions:

- fully pictorial and immediately obvious
- fully arbitrary and unrelated to visible signing

The better description is:

> SignWriting uses motivated visual resources within a conventional writing system

That means iconicity is uneven.

It varies by symbol class, use context, reader experience, teaching tradition, and text type.

Some hand symbols may be visually approachable.

Some movement conventions may need explicit instruction.

Some spatial placements may become clear only after a reader understands viewpoint and signbox conventions.

This unevenness is not a weakness in the typological argument.

It is part of the system's actual writing behavior.

It also prevents a second overclaim.

SignWriting's iconicity should not be treated as a guarantee of effortless literacy.

Motivation may support learning, memory, or initial orientation.

Those are plausible possibilities.

But they require evidence.

The writing-systems claim is narrower and stronger:

> whether or not iconicity makes learning easier in a measurable way, it does not disqualify SignWriting as writing

That keeps the argument where this paper can support it.

## From resemblance to written difference

The strongest test is not whether a symbol resembles something.

The strongest test is whether the system can make written differences.

A writing system must support contrast.

In SignWriting, contrast can appear through:

- choosing one handshape rather than another
- changing palm facing or rotation
- using a different movement arrow
- moving a symbol within the signbox
- adding or omitting facial/body information
- changing the relation among symbols
- changing sign boundaries or passage layout

Those are written differences.

They may correspond to differences in signed form, reading expectation, lexical identity, grammatical marking, or textual presentation depending on context.

The important point for this paper is narrower:

> visual motivation becomes writing when differences are conventional enough to be selected, read, corrected, and reused

This is why the phrase "just drawing" misses the point.

A drawing can be expressive without having a stable system of written contrast.

SignWriting depends on contrast.

This contrastive test is also where SignWriting differs from many pictorial or emoji-like uses.

A reader may interpret an emoji differently depending on context, tone, platform rendering, or local habit.

That flexibility can be communicatively powerful.

But it is not the same as a writing system in which recurring graphic distinctions are tied to a structured written form of language.

SignWriting allows interpretation too, but it also asks whether the writer selected the right symbol, orientation, placement, movement, relation, and boundary.

Those are not merely expressive options.

They are part of the written text.

## What convention does not solve by itself

Convention is necessary.

It is not magic.

A conventional system can still have open questions:

- Which distinctions matter most for fluent reading?
- Which symbol choices vary across writers or teaching traditions?
- How much spatial variation can readers tolerate?
- Which conventions are stable across signed languages, and which are local?
- Which forms are easy for beginners, and which require sustained instruction?
- How do handwritten forms differ from font-rendered forms?

These questions should be studied rather than hidden.

They belong partly in *Sutton SignWriting Language and Literacy Research*, partly in *Handwriting and Spatial Script Practice*, and partly in evidence-building around actual readers and texts.

The presence of open questions does not reduce SignWriting to illustration.

It means SignWriting should be studied as a real writing system.

Real writing systems have variation, learning curves, local norms, and histories of stabilization.

## Relation to plane-based writing

The first native paper in this series, *Plane-Based Writing and Script Typology*, defined plane-based writing as writing in which the primary written unit is composed through spatial relation within a two-dimensional field.

This paper adds an important condition:

> plane-based writing requires legible convention

A plane by itself is not writing.

A spatial arrangement by itself is not writing.

An iconic mark by itself is not writing.

Writing begins when selected forms and relations are stabilized enough for a reader to recover a durable written object.

For SignWriting, that means the signbox is not only a spatial field.

It is a conventional writing field.

The writer does not merely place marks wherever they seem visually pleasant.

The writer works inside a system of symbol choices, viewpoint conventions, relation conventions, and layout practices.

This is why iconicity and plane-based composition belong together.

The plane gives the system a spatial organization.

Convention makes that organization readable.

## Relation to authored space

The preceding paper, *Perspective and Authored Space*, argued that the writer authors viewpoint, placement, relation, and composed space.

This paper explains why that authorship can be legible.

Authored space does not mean private space.

It does not mean the writer invents a visual solution from nothing each time.

Authorship happens inside convention.

The writer chooses.

But the choices are meaningful because readers share the system.

That is true in all writing.

Alphabetic writers choose words, punctuation, capitalization, spacing, and sometimes layout. Those choices are authored, but they are intelligible because they take place inside shared conventions.

SignWriting writers choose symbols, viewpoint, placement, relation, and layout. Those choices are authored too, and they become legible because the system gives readers ways to interpret them.

Authorship and convention are not opposites.

Convention is what makes authorship readable.

## Relation to machine encoding

The following technical-bridge paper, *Human Writing and Machine Encoding*, asks how authored signed writing can be stored and processed by machines.

This paper prepares that question by clarifying what must be preserved.

Encoding does not need to preserve a vague visual impression.

It needs to preserve conventional written distinctions.

That includes:

- symbol identity
- orientation-relevant distinctions
- signbox placement
- sign boundaries
- movement and relation symbols
- enough structure for rendering, search, comparison, and reuse

If iconic writing were merely drawing, encoding would only need to store an image.

But if iconic writing is conventional writing, encoding must preserve the structured text behind the visible form.

That is why the distinction matters technically as well as theoretically.

## What this paper claims

This paper makes five claims.

First, iconicity is not the opposite of writing.

Second, visual motivation becomes writing when it is stabilized by shared conventions.

Third, SignWriting is not free illustration because its symbols and spatial relations participate in a learned system of contrast.

Fourth, legibility depends on trained recognition, not raw resemblance.

Fifth, convention makes authored spatial writing preservable, teachable, correctable, and technically supportable.

## What this paper does not claim

The paper does not claim that every SignWriting symbol is immediately transparent to novices.

It does not claim that iconicity automatically produces reading fluency.

It does not claim that all communities use or value the same conventions in exactly the same way.

It does not claim that every iconic or spatial symbol system is writing.

It does not claim that pictograms, emoji, maps, diagrams, or interface icons become writing merely because they are conventional.

It does not claim that the questions of handwriting, reader studies, or educational outcomes are already resolved.

Those questions remain important.

They should be handled in the writing-systems and research series where they belong.

## Conclusion

SignWriting should not be defended by denying its iconicity.

Its visual motivation is part of its representational strength.

The stronger argument is that iconicity alone is not the deciding category.

For writing, convention is what makes visual motivation legible as part of a shared system.

When motivated forms become stable, contrastive, teachable, correctable, and reusable, they can become writing.

That is what SignWriting demonstrates.

It shows that a writing system can preserve visible features of signed language without collapsing into free drawing.

It also shows that a plane-based writing system requires more than spatial placement.

It requires legible convention.

From here, the next question is technical but not merely technical:

> how can those conventional written distinctions be preserved as machine-readable text?

That is the bridge first to *Handwriting and Spatial Script Practice*, and then to *Human Writing and Machine Encoding*.
