# Kinematography and the Limits of Writing-System Typology

**Signed writing as a pressure test for grapholinguistics**

This paper is a native capstone of the `Writing and Representation in Sutton SignWriting` series.

It follows *Human Writing and Machine Encoding* and prepares the way for the comparative support entry, *Other Sign Language Scripts and Their Conceptions*.

The preceding papers have built a layered account:

- SignWriting is plane-based in organization
- the writer authors viewpoint, placement, relation, and composed space
- iconicity becomes writing through convention and legibility
- handwriting and material practice matter
- machine encoding should preserve authored written distinctions rather than replace them

This paper asks the capstone question:

> what should writing-system typology call a system that writes articulatory-spatial-kinetic form?

The signed writing system considered here is Sutton SignWriting.

The answer proposed here is cautious:

> `kinematography` may be a useful candidate term for writing whose primary representational work is the durable inscription of articulatory-spatial-kinetic form

This is not offered as settled field terminology.

It is offered as a pressure test.

The term is useful only if it does more than rename SignWriting.

It must clarify a general typological question:

> how should writing theory describe durable writing that represents the form of signed action rather than sound, lexical meaning alone, or abstract notation?

If a term like this becomes necessary to describe what SignWriting is doing structurally, then the problem is not only that SignWriting is difficult to classify.

The problem may be that current typology lacks a stable place for a real kind of writing.

## Why another term is even worth considering

Writing-system typology already has many useful terms.

It can name systems and tendencies such as:

- phonography
- morphography
- syllabography
- segmental writing
- featural writing
- notation
- pictography
- semasiography
- mixed systems

Those terms do real work.

The point of this paper is not to multiply terminology for its own sake.

The point is to ask whether the existing terms can describe signed writing without distorting it.

For SignWriting, several inherited terms each capture part of the picture.

None captures the whole.

That is the reason to consider a candidate term.

The question is not:

> can SignWriting be squeezed into an existing category?

The better question is:

> what category would describe SignWriting without erasing the structure that makes it work?

That question matters because typology is not only naming.

It shapes what scholars look for.

If SignWriting is treated mainly as pictography, the analysis will focus on resemblance.

If it is treated mainly as notation, the analysis will focus on specialist transcription.

If it is treated mainly as a featural script, the analysis will focus on inventory and decomposition.

Each of those views can reveal something.

Each can also hide the central fact that the written object preserves signed action as a composed readable form.

A useful term should help the reader see that central fact sooner.

## Why existing labels strain

Each familiar label gives a partial answer.

`Phonography` is too speech-shaped if it is understood primarily as writing sound.

Signed languages do have formational structure, and signs can be analyzed into parameters and features, but signed writing is not writing speech sounds.

`Morphography` or `logography` is too meaning-centered if it treats written signs mainly as marks for morphemes, words, or lexical meanings.

SignWriting may write lexical signs, but it does so by preserving written form, not by replacing signed form with meaning labels.

`Featural writing` is useful but too low-level by itself.

SignWriting represents handshape, movement, orientation, location, facial expression, and other features, but a written sign is not merely a list of features.

It is a composed written object.

`Pictography` or `iconography` overemphasizes resemblance.

SignWriting uses visually motivated resources, but it is not free drawing and not merely a set of pictures for ideas.

`Notation` is useful for some analytic uses, but too narrow if it implies a specialist transcription system rather than ordinary readable signed text.

`Semasiography` is misleading if it means signs point directly to ideas without writing a language form.

SignWriting writes signed language forms.

`Plane-based writing` is necessary but not sufficient.

It describes how the written unit is organized.

It does not by itself name what kind of linguistic form the writing primarily preserves.

The strain appears because these labels answer different questions.

They should not be forced to do one another's work.

## What kinematography would name

This paper uses `kinematography` as a candidate analytic term.

Definition:

> Kinematography is writing whose primary representational work is the durable inscription of articulatory-spatial-kinetic form.

This use is distinct from cinematography.

It is not about film, moving images, or recording motion with a camera.

It uses the root sense of movement to name a writing-theory problem: how durable visible writing can preserve the form of signed action.

The term has four parts.

`Articulatory` means that the writing is concerned with bodily form as language-bearing action: hands, face, body, orientation, contact, location, and related articulators.

`Spatial` means that relation, placement, viewpoint, and composed space are not merely later visual layout. They are part of the written form.

`Kinetic` means that movement, direction, path, repetition, timing cues, and dynamic relations are represented in durable form.

`Writing` means that the result is not raw movement, video, gesture, mime, or illustration. It is conventional, durable, readable symbolic text.

Used this way, kinematography does not mean any writing that mentions movement.

It does not mean any script with curved lines or dynamic-looking marks.

It means a writing type in which the structure being written is primarily articulatory, spatial, and kinetic.

SignWriting is the strongest case in this platform for that description.

## A minimum test for the term

The term should not be applied loosely.

For `kinematography` to be useful as a typological label, a writing system should meet several conditions.

First, it must write a language form, not merely label a meaning.

Second, its written distinctions must preserve bodily articulation as part of the linguistic form being written.

Third, spatial relation must carry written value, not merely page-layout value.

Fourth, movement or dynamic relation must be represented in a durable way rather than left entirely to memory, video, or oral explanation.

Fifth, the resulting text must be conventional enough to be taught, read, copied, searched, rendered, compared, and reused.

These conditions are intentionally stricter than resemblance.

A picture of a person signing is not kinematographic writing.

A video of signing is not kinematographic writing.

A label that glosses a sign with a spoken-language word is not kinematographic writing.

A linear analytic transcription may contain kinematic information, but it is not necessarily kinematographic as a writing system unless that information is organized as durable readable language text.

The point is not to police boundaries for their own sake.

The point is to keep the term from becoming a decorative synonym for "visual" or "movement-related."

## A worked typological contrast

Consider a written sign that contains a handshape symbol, an orientation distinction, a movement symbol, facial information, and placement in relation to the body.

A phonographic question would ask:

> what sound units does this writing represent?

That question misses the target.

A morphographic question would ask:

> what word, morpheme, or meaning does this written unit stand for?

That question may help at the lexical layer, but it does not explain why the internal written form matters.

A featural question would ask:

> which articulatory parameters are encoded?

That question is useful, but it can make the written sign look like a bundle of independent features.

A pictographic question would ask:

> what does the sign resemble?

That question may explain some visual motivation, but it cannot explain conventional legibility by itself.

A notational question would ask:

> how precisely does this system record an observed signing event for specialist analysis?

That question fits some uses, but it does not cover ordinary writing, reading, composition, or publication.

A kinematographic question asks:

> how does this written unit preserve signed articulatory-spatial-kinetic form as durable readable text?

That question does not replace the others.

It organizes them around the kind of writing being analyzed.

It asks the scholar to treat the written sign as a composed language-bearing object, not as a picture, a gloss, a code, or a list.

## Plane-based and kinematographic are different axes

This distinction is the key to keeping the term useful.

`Plane-based` describes the organizational axis.

It answers:

> how is the written unit organized?

`Kinematographic` describes a possible representational axis.

It answers:

> what kind of language form does the writing primarily preserve?

The terms do not compete.

They describe different things.

A system could use spatial organization without being kinematographic.

Mathematical notation and musical notation use spatial organization, but they do not write signed articulatory-spatial-kinetic language forms.

A system could represent movement without making the written unit plane-based in the SignWriting sense.

Linear transcription systems may encode movement categories without composing a signbox as the primary written unit.

For SignWriting, the two axes meet:

| Axis | Description in SignWriting |
| --- | --- |
| Organizational | plane-based signbox composition |
| Representational | articulatory-spatial-kinetic signed form |
| Material | handwritten, font-rendered, printed, digital, and encoded forms |
| Technical | Formal SignWriting, FSW, SWU, rendering, search, and layout |

This is why the series needed the earlier papers first.

Without plane-based writing, kinematography could sound like a vague movement metaphor.

Without authored space, it could sound like a list of body parts.

Without iconicity and convention, it could sound pictorial.

Without handwriting and material practice, it could sound abstract.

Without machine encoding, it could sound technically impossible.

The term becomes useful only after those layers are separated.

## What would count against the term

A mature typological proposal needs possible failure conditions.

Kinematography would be the wrong primary description if SignWriting were shown to work mainly as a semantic prompt rather than as writing of signed form.

It would be the wrong primary description if spatial placement turned out to be decorative page arrangement rather than part of written sign structure.

It would be the wrong primary description if movement symbols functioned only as optional illustration rather than as conventional written distinctions.

It would be the wrong primary description if ordinary users could read signs only by translating them into spoken-language glosses first.

It would also be too strong if the system were usable only as expert notation and not as durable text for reading, teaching, publication, or ordinary composition.

These are empirical and descriptive questions.

They cannot be settled by terminology alone.

The value of the term is that it points to the right evidence: reading practice, writing practice, teaching practice, corpus structure, rendering behavior, search behavior, and the stability of written distinctions across use.

## Why not just featural?

The featural label is the most tempting partial fit.

SignWriting symbols do represent internal features of signed articulation.

That matters.

But featural description is not enough.

Features are parts.

A written sign is a composed unit.

The writer does not merely record a handshape, a palm orientation, a movement type, and a location in a list.

The writer composes a written sign in which those elements stand in spatial relation.

That relation is part of the text.

A featural account can ask:

> which parts of signed form are represented?

A kinematographic account asks:

> what kind of embodied linguistic form is preserved as a readable written object?

Those questions overlap.

They are not identical.

Calling SignWriting featural may be accurate at one layer, but incomplete as a typological description of the whole writing system.

## Why not just pictographic?

The pictographic or iconographic label also captures something real but misleading.

SignWriting is visually motivated.

Some symbols resemble aspects of the body, movement, or spatial relation.

But the writing does not work by free resemblance.

It works through conventionalized written distinctions.

The previous paper, *Iconicity, Convention, and Legibility*, made that point directly:

> transparency is not the same as legibility

A kinematographic description therefore does not deny iconicity.

It locates iconicity inside a larger writing system.

The category is not resemblance.

The category is durable inscription of signed action through conventional written form.

## Why not just notation?

SignWriting can function as notation in some contexts.

It can be used for dictionaries, linguistic examples, transcription-like work, and corpus annotation.

But a writing system can have notational uses without being only notation.

The difference is functional and social as well as structural.

A specialist notation is usually designed for analytic precision in controlled contexts.

A writing system can be used for ordinary reading, teaching, composition, publication, and personal writing.

SignWriting has to be evaluated across that larger range.

That is why kinematography should not be understood as a new name for notation.

It is a proposed representational type for language writing.

## Why not semasiography?

Semasiography names visible meaning-making that does not necessarily write a language form.

That distinction is useful.

It keeps maps, pictograms, emoji, diagrams, and direct idea signs from being collapsed into language writing.

But it is not the right primary description for SignWriting.

SignWriting does not primarily bypass signed language to point directly at meanings.

It writes signed language forms in durable visible form.

That matters because signed languages are languages, not visual translations of spoken-language meaning.

If a written sign preserves handshape, movement, location, orientation, facial expression, and spatial relation, then its representational work is not merely semantic.

It is articulatory-spatial-kinetic.

That is the gap kinematography tries to name.

## What the term clarifies

Used carefully, kinematography clarifies several confusions.

It keeps SignWriting from being misread as speech writing.

It keeps SignWriting from being reduced to meaning labels.

It keeps SignWriting from being flattened into a feature list.

It keeps SignWriting from being dismissed as pictorial drawing.

It keeps SignWriting from being treated as only technical notation.

It also helps connect the human and technical layers.

If the writing is kinematographic in this sense, then the technical infrastructure must preserve:

- symbol identity
- spatial relation
- movement information
- orientation and contact distinctions
- sign boundaries
- enough structure for reading, search, rendering, comparison, and reuse

This explains why the Formal SignWriting model matters.

It is not only an encoding convenience.

It is a technical response to the problem of preserving articulatory-spatial-kinetic writing as text.

It also clarifies why the human-writing layer must remain visible.

If kinematography is only treated as data encoding, the typological claim collapses into a file-format claim.

If it is only treated as handwriting or visual art, the technical claim disappears.

The interesting case is the connection between the two:

> a human writer composes signed form in durable visible space, and a technical system preserves that composition as processable text

That connection is why this paper follows *Human Writing and Machine Encoding*.

The typology depends on keeping both halves in view.

## Relation to the Critical Review series

The term `kinematography` was raised earlier in the `Signed Language Writing Critical Review Series`, especially in the Meletis and Durscheid review and companion paper.

There, its role was diagnostic.

It helped ask whether a strong grapholinguistic framework could house signed writing without strain.

This paper gives the term a different role.

Here, it is developed inside the writing-systems lane as a native theoretical proposal.

The critical-review use asks:

> where does existing theory strain?

This paper asks:

> what analytic term might describe the structure more directly?

Both uses should remain cautious.

Neither turns kinematography into settled terminology.

Together, they make the same pressure visible from two directions: source critique and native theory-building.

## What this paper claims

This paper makes six claims.

First, SignWriting pressures writing-system typology because existing labels each capture only part of its structure.

Second, `plane-based` and `kinematographic` answer different questions: organization and representation.

Third, `kinematography` is a useful candidate term for writing whose primary representational work is articulatory, spatial, and kinetic.

Fourth, the term is useful only if it remains bounded by criteria: language form, bodily articulation, spatial relation, durable movement representation, and conventional readable text.

Fifth, the term helps avoid misleading reductions to phonography, morphography, featural description, pictography, notation, or semasiography.

Sixth, signed writing should be treated as a theory-shaping case for grapholinguistics, not as a marginal exception.

## What this paper does not claim

The paper does not claim that `kinematography` is settled field terminology.

It does not claim that grapholinguistics must adopt this exact word.

It does not claim that all signed writing must be SignWriting.

It does not claim that SignWriting captures every physical detail of signing.

It does not claim that video, notation, linguistic transcription, or semantic modeling are obsolete.

It does not claim that the typological question settles literacy outcomes, adoption, community preference, or educational effectiveness.

It does not claim that kinematography replaces plane-based writing.

The term is offered as a working proposal and a pressure test.

It also does not claim that the term is proven merely because it is useful here.

A stronger future version would test the term against additional signed-writing systems, pedagogical practices, handwritten materials, corpus data, and reader behavior.

## Conclusion

The Writing and Representation in Sutton SignWriting series began with a simple correction:

> writing does not have to be linear

It then added more precise claims:

- SignWriting is plane-based in organization
- authored space belongs to the written form
- iconicity becomes writing through convention
- handwriting and material practice matter
- machine encoding should preserve writing rather than redefine it

This paper adds the capstone question:

> what kind of representational type is signed writing?

If the answer cannot be stated cleanly with phonography, morphography, featural writing, pictography, notation, or semasiography, then the typology is being asked to grow.

`Kinematography` may or may not become the final term.

The pressure it names is real.

Signed writing asks grapholinguistics to describe writing whose primary representational work is articulatory, spatial, and kinetic.

If grapholinguistics cannot name that kind of writing, the problem is not only with SignWriting's classification.

It is with the descriptive reach of the typology itself.
