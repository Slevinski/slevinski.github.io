# Other Sign Language Scripts and Their Conceptions

**ASL-phabet, HamNoSys, ASLwrite, and what they imply about written sign language**

This entry is a comparative support paper in the `Writing and Representation in Sutton SignWriting` series.

A small number of other sign-language script conceptions should be acknowledged because a serious SignWriting writing-systems lane should acknowledge the visible landscape around it.

The point is not to produce a laundry list and not to turn every other system into an opponent.

The point is simpler:

- the field is not empty
- different systems embody different conceptions of what signed writing is for
- SignWriting should be presented through its own strengths rather than through a reactive argument against competitors

These systems also emerged from different communities and different practical needs.

Some were built around educational goals.

Some were built for linguistic notation.

Some were built around alternate script design and handwriting interests.

SignWriting has increasingly had to operate across all of those pressures while also becoming technical infrastructure.

## Why name other scripts at all

SignWriting should not be framed as if no one else has tried to represent signed language in written form.

A few systems are visible enough to deserve acknowledgment:

- **ASL-phabet**
- **HamNoSys**
- **ASLwrite**

Each one matters for a different reason.

Together they show that signed writing is an active intellectual and practical field rather than a private concern.

## Different conceptions of signed writing

The most useful distinction is not merely graphic.

It is functional.

Different systems answer different questions:

- Is signed writing mainly an **alphabetic teaching device**?
- Is it mainly **analytic notation** for research and transcription?
- Is it a **plane-based script** meant to be read and written directly?
- Is it part of a larger **infrastructure** of standards, software, datasets, and public continuity?

Those are not identical goals.

The systems below should be understood in relation to those goals.

They should also not be collapsed into a single category merely because they all concern signed language.

That is the main comparative point for this project: different scripts occupy different functional roles, while SignWriting is evaluated here primarily as plane-based writing with an unusually developed technical and public support path.

The comparison can be summarized this way:

| System | Main conception | Strongest layer | Does not primarily solve |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ASL-phabet | Alphabetic and pedagogical model for ASL | Teaching, feature mapping, and literacy-oriented word work | Full plane-based signed text infrastructure |
| HamNoSys | Analytic notation for sign forms | Linguistic transcription and cross-language notation | Everyday native-facing writing practice |
| ASLwrite | Community spatial script culture | Handwriting, community experimentation, and alternate plane-based design | Large technical ecosystem or standards infrastructure, at least as currently documented here |
| SignWriting | Plane-based writing with public and technical infrastructure | Writing, encoding, fonts, software, datasets, corpora, and stewardship | Universal adoption, settled outcomes evidence, or every local handwriting question |

## Institutional involvement, funding, and vision

The systems also differ in where they were built, how they were supported, and what kind of future they were trying to create.

### ASL-phabet

- **Institutional involvement**: associated with educational work in Arizona and scholarship around ASL literacy
- **Support structure**: educational and academic collaboration
- **Vision**: establish an alphabetic principle for ASL, support signed word reading, and help bridge toward written English

This is a specific educational vision.

It is not the same as building an enduring public writing ecosystem for signed language across software, standards, and long-term technical continuity.

### HamNoSys

- **Institutional involvement**: strongly academic in origin and long associated with the University of Hamburg
- **Support structure**: scholarly and research-oriented rather than community handwriting or public script adoption
- **Vision**: provide a detailed, internationally applicable notation system for representing sign forms across languages

That makes HamNoSys significant.

It also makes it a different kind of project from SignWriting.

### ASLwrite

- **Institutional involvement**: outside formal academic infrastructure
- **Support structure**: community-driven and socially sustained
- **Vision**: offer an alternate plane-based script with room for handwriting, everyday use, and a different visual culture of signed writing

That community independence is part of what makes ASLwrite interesting.

It is not trying to be a university notation system.

### SignWriting

- **Institutional involvement**: historically broader in use than in formal academic housing
- **Support structure**: international public use, independent stewardship, and growing need for durable institutional support
- **Vision**: function as a real writing system for signed languages while also supporting literacy, software, standards, corpus work, and long-term infrastructure

This is why SignWriting raises a different scale of question.

It is not only a script-design question.

It is also a question of how a writing system can remain stable, teachable, and broadly reusable over time.

## ASL-phabet

ASL-phabet is one visible linear alphabetic conception in US discourse.

Its importance here is not that it defines the future of signed writing.

Its importance is that it is visible enough in US educational and academic settings to warrant direct acknowledgment.

The key point is:

- it treats signed writing through a **linear alphabetic conception**
- it is tied to **literacy-oriented claims**
- it has often been framed around individual word representation and instructional use
- it is part of the current US conversation, whether or not one agrees with its design

That makes it part of the surrounding landscape.

It does not have to become the center of the SignWriting case.

## HamNoSys

HamNoSys is one of the best-known names in sign-language notation.

It represents a different conception from ordinary writable script.

Its strength is analytic representation.

That matters for:

- scholarly transcription
- representational precision
- comparative notation work

It is best understood as part of a linguistic notation tradition.

But it is not the same thing as a broad, native-facing writing system intended for ordinary reading, writing, educational use, and public literacy infrastructure.

That distinction should be made clearly and without hostility.

## ASLwrite

ASLwrite deserves acknowledgment as a second plane-based script.

That matters because it shows SignWriting is not the only serious attempt to treat signed writing spatially rather than only linearly.

ASLwrite is also valuable because it keeps the question of handwriting alive.

It is useful to understand ASLwrite as a community-driven alternate spatial script design rather than merely as a technical curiosity.

Its encoding approach is especially noteworthy:

- the visible script is plane-based
- some sign structure has been described through linearized or keyboardable forms
- its public materials emphasize writing practice, handwriting, and script culture more than standards infrastructure
- any stronger claim about its current software ecosystem should be documented separately before publication

This is a real script conception, not merely notation.

It is also a reminder that handwriting, legibility, and script culture remain live questions for signed writing.

## What SignWriting adds

SignWriting remains distinctive because it is more than a script idea in isolation.

It should also be described plainly as a formalized writing system, not merely as illustrations of signs.

It already exists as:

- a plane-based writing system for natural signed language
- a public computational model through Formal SignWriting, with FSW and SWU as canonical encodings
- a software and standards ecosystem
- a dataset and continuity problem worth institutional support
- a public technical record large enough to require stewardship

It also has its own handwriting tradition.

That tradition includes:

- shorthand
- cursive
- block printing

These traditions have appeared in pockets rather than through uniform large-scale adoption.

That should not be mistaken for failure.

It more likely reflects the historical fact that SignWriting's standardization and technical spread have outpaced the conditions needed for widespread handwriting culture.

That balance can change in the future.

## Standards note

`Sgnw` is the ISO 15924 script code for SignWriting.

That fact matters here only as a reminder that script identity and encoding strategy are different layers.

The detailed distinction between `Sgnw`, Formal SignWriting, FSW, and SWU belongs in `Formal SignWriting: FSW and SWU`.

## Why this belongs in the writing lane

Acknowledging these scripts makes several things easier to say clearly.

First, it shows that SignWriting exists in a real field rather than in isolation.

Second, it prevents the writing-systems lane from sounding naive or uninformed.

Third, it allows the SignWriting argument to remain constructive and accountable.

The central claim is not:

> every other script must fail before SignWriting can matter

The narrower claim is:

> SignWriting currently offers the most developed documented path in this platform for durable plane-based writing that can support computation, technical continuity, and broader public use

It is unusually complete because it spans:

- script design
- handwriting
- computational encoding
- standards
- software
- dataset growth
- continuity obligations

That is why this entry belongs in `Writing and Representation in Sutton SignWriting` rather than remaining only an infrastructure-side comparison note.

The claim should remain comparative rather than dismissive.

Other systems may be strongest for narrower purposes: pedagogy, transcription, handwriting culture, community experimentation, or research notation.

The writing-systems question is what each design makes easier to do and what it leaves for other layers to solve.

## Bottom line

Other sign-language scripts should be acknowledged fairly because they clarify the field.

ASL-phabet shows the continuing appeal of alphabetic teaching models.

HamNoSys shows the value of rigorous analytic notation.

ASLwrite shows that alternate plane-based script culture and handwriting remain live questions.

SignWriting shows a different kind of completeness: a plane-based writing system connected to alphabet stability, encodings, fonts, software, public texts, and long-term stewardship.

That does not make the other systems irrelevant.

It makes the comparison sharper.
