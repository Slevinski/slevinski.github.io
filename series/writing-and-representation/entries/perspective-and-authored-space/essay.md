# Perspective and Authored Space

**Viewpoint, placement, and the authored space of signed writing**

This paper develops the authored-space argument in *Writing and Representation in Sutton SignWriting*.

It follows *Plane-Based Writing and Script Typology*, but it does not repeat that paper's main argument.

The larger sequence begins with *The Shape of Writing*, which makes the broad claim:

> writing is not inherently linear

*Plane-Based Writing and Script Typology* then asks how SignWriting should be described once writing-system typology includes an organizational axis.

This paper asks the next question:

> if signed writing truly inhabits a plane, what does the writer actually author there?

The answer proposed here is that SignWriting is not spatial only in the abstract.

It is spatial in a stronger sense:

- viewpoint matters
- placement matters
- relation matters
- composition matters

This is what gives the writing-systems argument a firmer center than `point, line, plane` alone.

It is also what prepares the ground for *The Shape of a Sign*, where these questions become formalized in the signbox model.

## Where typology stops

*The Shape of Writing* argues that writing can work through points, lines, and planes.

That argument is necessary because it clears away a common assumption:

> real writing must unfold as a line

Once that assumption is removed, SignWriting becomes easier to recognize as writing.

But that first step is still broad.

*Plane-Based Writing and Script Typology* then gives that claim a more precise writing-systems frame: SignWriting needs both a representational account and an organizational account.

But even that typological step does not yet fully explain how authored spatial writing becomes legible, repeatable, and teachable.

That is where this paper begins.

The more concrete issue is not only that SignWriting uses space.

It is that the writer composes space in a disciplined way.

This means that spatial arrangement is not mere visual decoration added after the fact.

It belongs to the written form itself.

## Relation to writing-systems theory

This paper uses SignWriting to press on a familiar assumption in writing-systems theory.

Many typologies begin from systems whose primary organization is sequence: letters, syllabic signs, abjads, abugidas, morphographic characters arranged in lines, and mixed systems whose graphic units still move through text in ordered strings.

That literature is essential, but it can make spatial authorship look secondary.

SignWriting asks for a slightly different question.

Not only:

- what linguistic unit does a written mark represent?

But also:

- where is authored relation stored?
- how much spatial structure belongs to the written unit itself?
- when is placement a typographic consequence, and when is it part of the writing?

Those questions connect this paper to grapholinguistics, notation theory, and general writing-systems theory.

The claim is not that SignWriting escapes all existing categories.

The narrower claim is that a signed writing system exposes a pressure point in those categories: writing can be conventional, symbolic, stable, and language-bearing while giving spatial composition a structural role usually assigned to sequence.

## Authored viewpoint

Every writing system has to solve a representational problem.

It has to decide what counts as the written object and what counts as contextual interpretation around it.

In line-based systems, this problem is often easier to overlook because sequence does much of the work. Symbols follow one another, and readers learn conventions of order, grouping, and punctuation that stabilize the text.

In SignWriting, the problem becomes more visible because the writer is not only selecting symbols.

The writer is also deciding how a sign will be seen in writing.

That is why viewpoint belongs inside the theory of the writing system.

The question is not:

- what does a signer physically look like from every possible angle?

The question is:

- which authored viewpoint conventions allow a written sign to be produced, reproduced, and read?

This is a writing problem, not only a drawing problem.

A writer is not asked to recreate raw embodied vision in all of its perceptual richness.

A writer is asked to choose from a learned system of stable conventions.

That distinction matters because it separates SignWriting from both of the following:

- pure pictorial depiction
- purely analytic transcription

SignWriting is neither free illustration nor only researcher-facing markup.

It is authored visual-spatial writing.

## Front view, top view, and learned conventions

One reason spatial writing can seem unfamiliar is that people expect a single natural perspective.

But writing systems often depend on learned conventions that become unremarkable once literacy is established.

SignWriting makes this unusually visible.

Some symbols and arrangements are naturally understood through a front-facing convention.

Others become clearer through an overhead or top-down convention.

The issue is not whether one of these is the "real" view.

The issue is whether readers and writers can learn stable perspective conventions that support legibility.

They can.

This is one of the strongest reasons not to dismiss SignWriting as naive depiction. The writing system does not rely on uncontrolled visual guesswork. It relies on an authored set of conventions that can be taught, recognized, and reused.

That is no stranger in principle than other writing systems asking readers to learn:

- arbitrary character shapes
- stroke orders
- ligatures
- stacked vowel markers
- cursive joins
- punctuation conventions

What differs is the dimension in which those conventions operate.

In SignWriting, some of the learned discipline lies in how viewpoint is stabilized across a written sign.

The conventions are not created anew by each writer.

They are learned through SignWriting instruction, symbol reference materials, and ordinary reading practice. In modern Sutton SignWriting, the ISWA 2010 symbol inventory helps stabilize symbol identity, while SignWriting teaching materials and actual texts stabilize how those symbols are read in relation to a body-centered writing space.

That distinction matters.

ISWA 2010 does not by itself solve every writing-systems question. It is an alphabet and symbol-reference layer, not a full theory of reading. But without a stable alphabet, perspective conventions would have much less chance of becoming durable.

The front-view/top-view issue is therefore not a loose artistic preference.

It is part of a learned representational discipline:

- front-facing arrangements help anchor face, torso, and many body-centered relations
- top-view conventions help represent spatial relations that become clearer from above
- orientation and palm-facing distinctions help writers show how hands are turned
- movement symbols help readers recover motion without requiring moving images

There may be variation across teachers, communities, genres, and signed languages.

That variation should be studied rather than ignored.

The important point for this paper is that variation does not reduce the system to guesswork. Writing systems routinely stabilize practice through a mix of formal inventories, teaching traditions, reader habits, and local norms.

## Depth, suggestion, and learned perception

A second challenge appears quickly.

Signed language is not flat in the everyday physical sense.

Bodies move in three-dimensional space, and signers also use orientation, reach, and relative placement in ways that are not exhausted by a simple front-on image.

This creates an understandable worry:

> if signing happens in three dimensions, how can writing in two dimensions be adequate?

The answer is that writing rarely reproduces raw physical reality directly.

Writing selects, stabilizes, and suggests.

That is true in line-based systems and it remains true here.

SignWriting does not need to encode every possible spatial nuance as literal three-dimensional geometry.

It needs conventions that allow readers to recover meaningful spatial relations from a structured written form.

That means the writing selects some features and leaves others outside the written object.

It selects, for example:

- handshape
- location
- orientation
- movement path
- contact or near-contact where symbolized
- relative placement inside the signbox
- selected facial and body information

It does not attempt to preserve:

- every micro-movement of an actual signer
- every physical angle available to a camera
- continuous timing in the manner of video
- every gradient variation in speed, force, or expression

This is not a defect unique to SignWriting.

All writing systems reduce.

Alphabetic writing does not preserve every acoustic feature of speech. Musical notation does not preserve every performance detail. Mathematical notation does not preserve every mental step used to reach an expression.

The question is not whether a written system preserves everything.

The question is whether it preserves enough of the right structure for readers and writers to use it as writing.

That recovery is partly learned.

Readers become able to interpret:

- relative position
- symbol orientation
- spatial grouping
- movement cues
- authored viewpoint

This should not be overstated as a neuroscientific claim about perception.

The narrower and safer point is enough:

> readers can learn to interpret a disciplined visual-spatial writing system without requiring the writing to become raw 3D depiction

That is a normal property of literacy.

Written forms do not have to be identical to perception in order to be legible.

They have to be conventional enough, stable enough, and practiced enough.

## Authored relation and symbol placement

The strongest claim of this paper is that symbol placement is part of the writing itself.

In many writing systems, placement is largely downstream from sequence. A writer gives the symbols in order, and typography or layout handles much of the final presentation.

That is not what happens in SignWriting.

The writer does not merely list parts and let the page infer their relation.

The writer composes relation directly.

This means that the written sign carries meaning through:

- symbol identity
- symbol position
- symbol relation
- grouped composition

That composition is authored.

It is not just an illustration attached to language after the fact.

This is why spatial composition should be treated as semantically serious. If symbol relation were incidental, then many of the defining features of written signs would collapse into unordered inventories of parts. But that is not how actual signs are written or read.

The meaningful unit is not only the symbol list.

The meaningful unit is the composed sign.

Several ordinary cases make the point clear.

First, location matters.

A handshape and movement written near the forehead is not equivalent to the same handshape and movement written at the chest. The signbox does not merely decorate the symbol list; it records where the written action is anchored.

Second, relation matters.

If a contact symbol is placed between a hand and the face, it does different written work than if the same symbol is placed between two hands. The symbol identity alone is not enough. The relation created by placement is part of the written form.

Third, orientation matters.

Two hand symbols may use the same base handshape while differing in palm facing or rotation. Those differences are not font variants. They are part of the written contrast.

Fourth, grouping matters.

When movement arrows, hands, face symbols, and body-location symbols are composed together, the signbox gives the reader a structured visual field. It tells the reader which elements belong together and how they should be read in relation.

These examples are deliberately generic because this paper is about the writing-system level rather than a lexical analysis of particular signs.

The larger principle is simple:

> in SignWriting, the written sign is the composed spatial object, not an unordered bag of symbols

This is exactly where SignWriting differs most strongly from approaches that treat sign notation as decomposed analysis first and visible writing second.

SignWriting keeps the composed written object in view.

## Why this is still writing

At this point a familiar objection can reappear:

> if placement, viewpoint, and relation matter this much, isn't this closer to diagram or notation than to writing?

That objection only works if writing has already been defined too narrowly.

If writing is assumed to be sequence-first, then any stronger use of space will seem marginal. But that is the assumption *The Shape of Writing* was written to loosen.

Once writing is understood more broadly as durable symbolic language authored in stable conventional forms, SignWriting becomes easier to classify.

It is durable.

It is symbolic.

It is learnable.

It is reproducible.

It is writable and readable.

And crucially, its spatiality is not accidental.

It belongs to the form of the writing.

That does not make every visual notation into a writing system.

It means that a writing system may legitimately assign part of its structure to authored space rather than to sequence alone.

## The bridge to the signbox

This paper remains conceptual.

It is not a technical specification, and it should not try to become one.

But it should make the next step unavoidable.

If viewpoint, placement, and relation are part of authored writing, then the technical model cannot treat them as disposable rendering detail.

The system needs a way to preserve composed space in text.

That is the role of the signbox.

The signbox is where the writing system's spatial commitments become formally explicit.

It preserves:

- selected symbols
- their placement
- their relation inside a bounded written object

So this paper's bridge claim is simple:

> the signbox is not an arbitrary engineering trick added to visual writing

It is the formal consequence of taking authored spatial writing seriously.

Once that is clear, *The Shape of a Sign* can be read not as a detached technical note, but as the next layer of the same argument.

## The bridge to passage layout

The same point extends beyond the individual signbox.

If spatial relation belongs to the written form, then passage layout is not merely decoration around the text. It can affect how readers stay oriented to the composed space of each sign as they move through a passage.

The history and reader-response question around vertical writing belongs primarily in *Sutton SignWriting Language and Literacy Research: Reading Signed Text*.

This paper only needs the writing-systems point: passage layout can support or disrupt the reader's orientation to authored space.

This gives the writing-systems series a useful bridge:

> authored space exists inside the signbox, and passage layout helps readers carry that space across text

The formal details belong in *Sign Text Layout*. The conceptual importance belongs here too.

## What this paper does and does not claim

This paper claims:

- SignWriting uses authored space as part of writing itself
- viewpoint conventions are part of how spatial writing becomes legible
- passage layout can help readers maintain orientation to authored space
- readers can learn stable spatial conventions without requiring raw 3D depiction
- symbol placement and relation are semantically important
- these facts prepare the ground for the signbox model

This paper does not claim:

- that every perceptual issue has already been scientifically resolved
- that perspective conventions are culturally identical everywhere
- that the writing-systems argument can replace the formal technical model
- that spatial writing is simple merely because it is visible
- that this paper replaces the need for rendered examples, reader studies, or fuller grapholinguistic treatment

Its purpose is narrower.

It is meant to show that SignWriting inhabits authored space in a disciplined and writable way.

## Why this paper belongs after typology

The relation among the first anchors of this series is:

- *The Shape of Writing* asks whether writing must be linear
- *Plane-Based Writing and Script Typology* asks how SignWriting fits when typology includes the organization of written units
- *Perspective and Authored Space* asks what the writer actually authors once writing is allowed to inhabit a plane

The first paper opens the terrain.

The second paper gives that terrain a typological frame.

This paper makes that terrain more concrete.

Together they allow the writing-systems series to stand on its own rather than depending on one broad conceptual intervention followed only by technical notes.

## Conclusion

The central insight of this paper is modest but important.

SignWriting is not only a writing system that happens to use space.

It is a writing system in which authored viewpoint, spatial relation, and composed placement belong to the written form itself.

That is why the writing question cannot end with "plane-based" as a descriptive label.

It has to continue into the more concrete questions of:

- how viewpoint is stabilized
- how spatial relation becomes legible
- how passage layout supports reader orientation
- how placement carries meaning
- how composed written objects are preserved technically

Those are the questions that move the writing-systems series from abstract permission to real structure.

That is also why this paper belongs between *Plane-Based Writing and Script Typology* and the formal entries on *The Shape of a Sign* and *Sign Text Layout*.

From here, the strongest next moves are:

- *Iconicity, Convention, and Legibility* for the bridge from authored visual-spatial form to disciplined writing
- *Handwriting and Spatial Script Practice* for the bridge from legible convention to human material practice
- *Human Writing and Machine Encoding* for the bridge from authored written form to machine-readable text
- *The Shape of a Sign* for the formal technical bridge
- *Sign Text Layout* for vertical writing, horizontal writing, lanes, centering, and reading levels
- *Other Sign Language Scripts and Their Conceptions* for the comparative support layer
