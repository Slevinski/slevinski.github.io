# Plane-Based Writing and Script Typology

**Where SignWriting fits, and where existing writing-system categories strain**

This paper is the typological anchor of *Writing and Representation in Sutton SignWriting*.

It follows *The Shape of Writing* and prepares the ground for *Perspective and Authored Space*.

The earlier foundations paper makes a broad public claim:

> writing does not have to be linear

This paper asks the next, more technical writing-systems question:

> if writing does not have to be linear, how should a plane-based signed writing system be placed in script typology?

The answer is not that existing typologies are useless.

The signed writing system considered here is Sutton SignWriting.

Existing typologies are useful.

They give the field important vocabulary for alphabetic, syllabic, morphographic, featural, notational, and mixed systems.

But SignWriting exposes a limit in the usual question.

Typologies often ask what linguistic unit a written mark represents.

SignWriting also forces a second question:

> how is the primary written unit organized for reading?

That second question is the center of this paper.

## The typology problem

A writing system can be difficult to classify for two very different reasons.

One reason is weakness in the system itself.

The system may lack stable signs, consistent conventions, teachable structure, public use, or durable infrastructure.

Another reason is weakness in the classification question.

The system may be doing something real, but the available categories may have been shaped around a narrower sample of writing traditions.

SignWriting creates this second kind of problem.

It is not hard to classify because it lacks written structure.

It is hard to classify because much of its written structure appears where many typologies have not placed their main attention: inside a composed spatial unit.

A written sign is not merely a mark for a sound, a syllable, a word, or a morpheme.

It is also a structured written object.

The writer selects symbols and arranges them inside a signbox. That arrangement is not decorative typography added after the writing has already happened. It is part of the written form.

This creates a typological pressure point.

If the classification of writing systems only asks:

- does this system represent speech sounds?
- does it represent syllables?
- does it represent morphemes?
- does it represent words?
- does it represent meaning?

then signed writing will be pulled toward categories that do not fully describe what it is doing.

A better question has two parts:

- What does the writing represent?
- How is the written unit organized?

SignWriting needs both questions.

## Existing typology still matters

This paper does not reject writing-system typology.

It depends on it.

Work associated with grammatology, script typology, notation theory, and grapholinguistics has made several distinctions that remain useful here.

Daniels and Bright give writing-system study a wide comparative base across historical and contemporary scripts.

Coulmas and Sampson help keep the relation between writing, language, sound, meaning, and script type analytically visible.

Goodman's work on notation is useful because it asks when a visible mark system becomes sufficiently articulated, repeatable, and shareable to support stable reference.

Meletis and Durscheid's grapholinguistic account is especially important because it treats writing as a structured object of study rather than as a mere shadow of speech, while also showing how difficult typology remains.

The present paper builds from that literature rather than setting it aside.

The useful inherited distinctions include:

- writing is not identical to speech
- scripts can be classified by their relation to linguistic units
- many writing systems are mixed rather than pure
- written form has its own internal structure
- materiality, layout, and use matter
- typologies are tools, not final truths

Those distinctions matter because they prevent two bad shortcuts.

The first shortcut is to say:

> SignWriting is visual, so it is not really writing

The second shortcut is to say:

> SignWriting is writing, so it must fit neatly into an existing speech-shaped category

Both shortcuts fail.

The first denies the writing before studying it.

The second accepts the writing but misdescribes it.

A stronger account should preserve what existing typology already does well while adding an axis that signed writing makes necessary.

That addition should be understood as an extension, not a rejection.

The claim is not:

> typology has failed because SignWriting exists

The claim is:

> typology becomes more accurate when it asks not only what writing represents, but also how written units are organized

## The missing axis: organization

Many familiar classifications focus on representational correspondence.

They ask what kind of linguistic unit is associated with written marks:

| Common typological question | Examples of answers |
| --- | --- |
| What does the script mainly represent? | phonemes, syllables, morphemes, words, semantic units, features |
| How directly does writing map to language? | shallow, deep, mixed, morphophonemic, logographic, phonographic |
| What kind of sign inventory is used? | alphabet, abjad, abugida, syllabary, character system, notation system |

Those questions are useful.

They are not enough.

A writing system also has an organizational basis.

That basis asks how written units are arranged into readable form:

| Organizational question | Examples of answers |
| --- | --- |
| Is the primary written unit a point, line, block, cluster, plane, staff, diagram, or page? | alphabetic words, Chinese characters, mathematical formulas, musical staffs, SignWriting signboxes |
| Does placement belong to typography or to the authored written unit? | line breaking in prose versus symbol placement inside a signbox |
| Is spatial relation recoverable from the written text? | yes in plane-based composition; often no in a simple list of symbols |
| Does reading depend mainly on sequence, grouping, spatial relation, or some mixture? | sequence-dominant scripts, block systems, formulaic notation, spatial signed writing |

This organizational axis is not a replacement for representational typology.

It is an additional axis.

For many alphabetic scripts, the representational and organizational answers seem to move together:

- the script is phonographic
- the text is mostly linear
- the encoded order often resembles the reading order

That combination is common enough that it can start to look natural or universal.

It is not universal.

Some writing and notation systems already complicate it.

Mathematical notation uses superscripts, fractions, radicals, matrices, diagrams, and spatial alignment.

Musical notation organizes pitch, duration, voice, staff position, measure, and performance instruction across a visual field.

Chinese characters can be composed internally as square graphic units while also participating in line-based text.

Manuscript layout, tables, diagrams, and scientific notation all show that written meaning can depend on more than sequence.

SignWriting makes this organizational question primary rather than occasional.

That difference is important.

Mathematics and music show that spatial organization can carry disciplined meaning.

Chinese character structure shows that written units can have internal graphic organization while also participating in line-based text.

Tables, diagrams, and manuscript layout show that page structure can guide reading.

But these examples do not automatically become the same kind of object as SignWriting.

They are boundary tests.

They show that sequence is not the only organizing principle available to writing and notation, while also forcing a sharper question:

> when does spatial organization belong to the language-writing unit itself, rather than to a formula, score, diagram, page design, or secondary layout?

For SignWriting, the answer is unusually direct.

Spatial organization belongs inside the written sign.

That is why the signbox is typologically relevant.

## Defining plane-based writing

This paper uses `plane-based writing` as an analytic descriptor.

It is not presented as an established field category.

It is a proposed way to name what SignWriting makes visible.

Definition:

> Plane-based writing is writing in which the primary written unit is composed through spatial relation within a two-dimensional field, and where that spatial relation is part of the authored written form rather than only a downstream layout effect.

This definition has several important limits.

It does not mean that every mark placed on a page is plane-based writing.

All writing appears somewhere.

Alphabetic text is printed on a page, but the page location of each letter is usually not part of the authored structure of the word. It is mostly a consequence of typography, line breaking, font metrics, and layout.

Plane-based writing is different.

In plane-based writing, internal placement belongs to the written unit itself.

For SignWriting, this means the signbox matters.

The signbox is not merely a bounding rectangle around a picture.

It is the written field in which symbols are selected, placed, oriented, and related.

The spatial arrangement is part of the sign's written identity.

This also does not mean that plane-based writing is unconstrained.

A plane is not a free-for-all.

Plane-based writing still depends on:

- stable symbols
- learned conventions
- recurring graphic relations
- teachable structure
- reader expectations
- boundaries around written units
- ways to preserve, render, and compare written forms

In SignWriting, the plane is authored.

It is not accidental background.

It is also not the whole page.

The primary plane-based unit in SignWriting is the written sign inside its signbox.

Passage layout then arranges those written signs into lines, columns, lanes, and pages.

That distinction prevents another possible confusion.

Plane-based writing does not mean that every page-level layout choice is part of each written sign.

It means the internal spatial composition of the written sign is part of the writing system.

## A two-axis typology

The most useful move is to separate two questions.

Axis 1:

> What kind of language or symbolic structure does the writing preserve?

Axis 2:

> How is the written unit organized for reading?

This creates a more flexible typology.

| System or notation type | Representational basis | Organizational basis |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Alphabetic writing | mostly phonemic or segmental | mostly linear sequence |
| Abjads and abugidas | consonantal or consonant-vowel structure | mostly linear sequence with internal mark relations |
| Syllabaries | syllables | mostly linear sequence |
| Chinese characters | morphosyllabic and lexical structure | character block plus line-based text |
| Mathematical notation | symbolic, relational, quantitative structure | line, stack, plane, diagram, and page |
| Musical notation | pitch, duration, rhythm, performance instruction | staff-based spatial field over time |
| Linguistic transcription | analytic linguistic categories | usually linear with diacritics and grouping |
| HamNoSys | analytic sign-form notation | mainly sequence-oriented notation |
| SignWriting | articulatory-spatial signed form as written language | signbox-based plane composition plus passage layout |

This table is not meant to erase differences among these systems.

It is meant to show why one axis is not enough.

It also avoids a false choice.

SignWriting can be partly featural in what its symbols preserve, partly iconic in the motivation of some forms, partly notational in some analytic uses, and still be plane-based in the organization of its primary written unit.

Those descriptions answer different questions.

They should not be forced to compete as if only one label can be true.

SignWriting is not simply another symbol inventory to be inserted into a linear script typology.

It is a system in which the organization of the primary written unit is itself typologically important.

## Why SignWriting is not merely pictorial

The most common misunderstanding is to treat SignWriting as drawing.

That mistake is understandable.

SignWriting looks visually motivated. It uses symbols for hands, movement, facial expression, body location, and spatial relation. A reader can often see why a symbol has the shape it has.

But visual motivation does not make something non-writing.

Many writing systems contain motivated forms, historic iconicity, graphic analogy, or visible relation between form and meaning.

The question is not whether a form is visually motivated.

The question is whether it is conventionalized enough to function as writing.

SignWriting is not free drawing because it uses:

- a stable symbol inventory
- defined symbol classes
- learned orientation and fill distinctions
- recurring spatial conventions
- sign boundaries
- passage layout practices
- computational encodings that preserve written structure

A drawing can be interpreted.

A written sign can be read, copied, encoded, searched, corrected, compared, and taught as part of a shared system.

The difference is not that writing lacks visual resemblance.

The difference is that writing stabilizes resemblance through convention.

That is the bridge to *Iconicity, Convention, and Legibility*.

## Why SignWriting is not merely featural

Another near miss is to call SignWriting a featural script.

There is something useful in that instinct.

SignWriting does represent internal features of signed articulation:

- handshape
- palm facing
- orientation
- location
- movement
- facial and body information

But `featural` is not enough.

A list of features is not yet a written sign.

The sign is composed.

The writer does not merely say that a sign contains a handshape, a movement, and a facial expression. The writer arranges selected symbols in a spatial field so the reader can recover a written form.

That means SignWriting has featural elements, but its primary written unit is not simply a feature bundle.

It is a plane-composed written sign.

This distinction matters because it prevents a common reduction:

> if the symbols name articulatory features, then the writing system is just a feature notation

No.

The symbols matter, but the composed signbox matters too.

The typological description must account for both.

## Why SignWriting is not merely notation

SignWriting can be used analytically.

It can support dictionaries, corpora, search, comparison, and linguistic study.

But that does not make it only notation.

Notation systems often serve specialists who need precise, controlled representations for analysis.

Writing systems can also be used by ordinary readers and writers to produce durable texts.

The boundary is not always sharp.

Musical notation, mathematical notation, linguistic transcription, and dance notation all show that notation and writing overlap in complex ways.

The important question is functional:

> is the system only a specialist analytic code, or can it serve as readable written language?

SignWriting is designed and used as readable signed text, not only as a researcher's transcription system.

The evidence for public use, educational use, and institutional visibility is not developed in this paper.

That evidence belongs in *Sutton SignWriting Evidence and Adoption*, especially the entries on Brazil, South Korea, and the current evidence base.

The point here is typological:

> once a system is intended and used as readable signed text, it should not be reduced to specialist notation merely because it can also support analysis

That is why the typological account should not stop at notation.

It should describe a writing system that also supports notational, computational, educational, and analytic work.

## Why SignWriting is not merely encoding

A third mistake appears when SignWriting enters software.

Because Formal SignWriting, FSW, SWU, fonts, SVG, and Unicode are technically complex, readers may mistake the machine layer for the writing system.

That reverses the order.

The writing comes first.

The encoding preserves and processes the writing.

Formal SignWriting is important because it gives the authored written form a durable technical model:

- a temporal prefix where sorting sequence can be stored
- a spatial signbox where the written composition is preserved
- symbol identity
- coordinates
- sign boundaries
- conversion between FSW and SWU
- rendering paths
- search and comparison support

But the existence of an encoding does not mean the writing has become a line-based code.

Serialization is a machine requirement.

Plane-based composition is a writing-system property.

That distinction is developed later in this series, especially in *Human Writing and Machine Encoding*.

## What SignWriting contributes to typology

SignWriting contributes three important pressures to writing-system typology.

First, it shows that modality matters.

Signed languages are not spoken languages transferred into the hands.

They are natural languages in a visual-spatial modality.

A writing system for signed language should not be expected to look like a writing system for speech.

Second, it shows that organization matters.

If a writing system preserves spatially organized signed form, then the written unit may need to be spatially organized too.

Sequence alone is not the only possible organizing principle for writing.

Third, it shows that authored relation matters.

In SignWriting, placement is not always a typographic afterthought.

Placement can be part of the written sign.

That is what turns the signbox into a typological issue rather than a software convenience.

Together, these pressures suggest that writing-system typology should distinguish:

- representational type
- organizational type
- material and rendering type
- use type
- encoding and infrastructure type

Those layers interact, but they are not the same.

This is where SignWriting differs from a merely exotic example.

It does not only add another case to an existing list of scripts.

It asks whether the list itself has been organized around scripts whose main written units are easier to describe as sequences.

If so, signed writing is not a marginal exception.

It is a diagnostic case.

It shows where the typology needs another dimension.

## A typological description of SignWriting

Within this series, a careful description of SignWriting would be:

> SignWriting is a plane-based writing system for signed languages whose written signs are composed from a stable symbol inventory within bounded signboxes and arranged into passages through learned layout conventions.

That sentence is deliberately layered.

`SignWriting` identifies the tradition being described in this series.

`Plane-based writing system` identifies the organizational claim.

`For signed languages` identifies the language modality.

`Written signs` identifies the primary written unit.

`Stable symbol inventory` identifies the alphabet layer.

`Bounded signboxes` identifies the internal field of composition.

`Passages` and `layout conventions` identify how signs move beyond isolated units into text.

No single inherited label does all of that work.

That is why the description has to be built.

## What this paper claims

This paper makes five claims.

First, SignWriting should be treated as writing, not as illustration.

Second, its typological description requires both a representational axis and an organizational axis.

Third, `plane-based writing` is a useful analytic descriptor for systems whose primary written units are composed through authored spatial relation.

Fourth, the signbox is typologically significant because it is where written spatial composition is authored and preserved.

Fifth, existing writing-system typologies should be extended rather than discarded.

The goal is not to replace alphabetic, syllabic, morphographic, featural, or notational categories.

The goal is to avoid forcing signed writing into categories that were not built to see it clearly.

## What this paper does not claim

The paper does not claim that `plane-based writing` is already settled field terminology.

It is a platform analytic term offered for scrutiny and refinement.

The paper does not claim that all signed writing must be SignWriting.

Other systems may answer different questions or serve different communities.

The paper does not claim that SignWriting is superior to other writing systems because it is spatial.

It claims that its spatial organization must be described accurately before fair comparison is possible.

The paper does not claim that every spatial notation is language writing.

Maps, diagrams, dance notation, mathematical notation, and musical notation all use space, but they do not all have the same relation to language.

The paper does not claim that this typological argument proves literacy outcomes.

Reading fluency, educational effects, adoption patterns, and community use require their own evidence.

Those questions belong primarily in *Sutton SignWriting Language and Literacy Research* and *Sutton SignWriting Evidence and Adoption*.

The paper also does not claim that the proposed two-axis account is final.

It is a working typological proposal.

Future papers on kinematic writing may refine the terms used here.

## Why this belongs first in the writing-systems series

This paper belongs near the front of *Writing and Representation in Sutton SignWriting* because it changes the question the rest of the series answers.

Without this paper, the series can seem to be saying:

> SignWriting is unusual, and here are some unusual properties

With this paper, the series can say something stronger:

> SignWriting exposes a missing organizational axis in writing-system description

That does not finish the argument.

It starts it properly.

The following entries then have clearer jobs:

- *Perspective and Authored Space* explains what the writer authors inside the plane.
- *Iconicity, Convention, and Legibility* explains how motivated visual form becomes disciplined writing.
- *Handwriting and Spatial Script Practice* explains how a spatial script lives in human material practice.
- *Human Writing and Machine Encoding* explains how a plane-composed written unit can become machine-processable text without becoming line-based writing.
- *The Shape of a Sign* in *Formal SignWriting* formalizes the signbox, symbol identity, coordinates, and boundaries.
- *Other Sign Language Scripts and Their Conceptions* compares neighboring script conceptions without making them carry the main typological argument.

## Conclusion

SignWriting does not ask writing-system typology to abandon its existing categories.

It asks typology to add a question that should have been explicit all along:

> how is the written unit organized?

For many scripts, the answer is mostly linear sequence.

For SignWriting, the answer is different.

The written sign is composed in a plane.

That does not make it less like writing.

It makes visible a dimension of writing that line-based systems often allow us to ignore.

Plane-based writing is therefore not a slogan.

It is a proposed typological descriptor for a real structural fact:

> in SignWriting, authored spatial relation belongs to the written form itself

That is the point from which the rest of the writing-systems series can proceed.
